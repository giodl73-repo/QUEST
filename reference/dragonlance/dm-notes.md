# DM Notes — Writing Dragonlance

Dragonlance is not generic fantasy. It has a specific emotional register that our adventures should hit. Use these notes as a tone guide.

## The Five Recurring Themes

### 1. Fallen Grandeur
Dragonlance is obsessed with ruin. Istar — the height of human civilization — is the thing that broke the world by demanding too much from the gods. The Cataclysm is the central wound. **In the Age of Might (our adventure's setting), the characters don't yet know this is coming.** There should be a subtle feeling of the audience knowing more than the characters — a doomed beauty.

**Writing note:** every grand thing in Dragonlance is on its way to being lost. Good writing lets the player feel this without the DM narrating it.

### 2. The Gods Test Mortals
Dragonlance's gods are neither Olympian caricatures nor D&D-generic stat blocks. They *care*. Paladine tests; Takhisis tempts; Mishakal heals; Gilean observes. The gods' absence in the Age of Despair is their most devastating punishment — not wrath, but silence.

**Writing note:** divine intervention is rare, meaningful, and earned. A paladin's smite is cosmic, not mechanical.

### 3. The Price of Power
Every source of magic in Dragonlance costs something. The Test of High Sorcery literally kills wizards. Clerical magic requires *genuine* devotion — not a class feature but a contract with a god who can revoke it (and did, for an entire age). **Artifacts always cost.** This is why the cursed-artifact treasure philosophy fits Dragonlance perfectly.

**Writing note:** when we write a magic item, ask "what does this cost?" — the answer is never "nothing."

### 4. Heroism Is Ordinary People Bearing Unbearable Weight
The Heroes of the Lance were not Epic Chosen Ones. They were a half-elf, a dwarf, a pair of kender, a knight past his prime, a hill-barbarian woman, a failed wizard, and a thief. Dragonlance heroes are defined by their *flaws* — Sturm's rigidity, Raistlin's bitterness, Tanis's indecision. They succeed because they keep showing up.

**Writing note:** NPCs should have a weight they're carrying. Our archivist in #0001 is carrying the Order's shame. The dead knight whose tomb we're raiding was carrying grief.

### 5. Loss as Narrative Engine
Dragonlance moves by loss. Loss of the gods, loss of Istar, loss of family (Raistlin/Caramon, Goldmoon/Riverwind), loss of the world that was. A Dragonlance adventure that doesn't cost the party something has missed the setting.

**Writing note:** whatever the party takes home from our dungeons, they lose something too.

## Prose Style

- **Elegiac, not bombastic.** Dragonlance prose (Weis & Hickman in particular) leans toward the spare and ritual. Avoid "adjective-adjective NOUN!" pile-ups.
- **Nouns over adjectives.** "A rose of moon-silver" lands harder than "a gleaming, intricate, silvery rose."
- **Let silence speak.** A read-aloud that ends before it needs to is stronger than one that over-explains.
- **Weather is character.** Krynn's weather is always doing something — wind across the Plains, cold in the Kharolis, a deadness in the air near Istar's ruins. Use weather to set mood.

## Names & Vocabulary

Use setting-native terms:
- "Solamnic" not "knightly"
- "Krynnish" not "worldly"
- "Cataclysm" capitalized, always
- "Measure" and "Oath" (Solamnic) capitalized
- Gods' names spelled: Paladine, Takhisis, Mishakal, Gilean, Solinari, Lunitari, Nuitari
- Moons: "silver" (Solinari), "red" (Lunitari), "black" (Nuitari)

## Things to Avoid

- Generic "tavern in a fantasy kingdom" openings. Use a specific Abanasinian way-house, a Solamnic waystation, a Qualinesti glade — not "The Prancing Pony."
- Hero's-journey inevitabilities. Dragonlance heroes usually fail at half their tasks.
- Loot as pure reward. Every artifact is a wound that hasn't healed. (This is the workshop's north star anyway.)
- Paladin-stereotypes from other fantasy. A Solamnic Knight is *legalistic, burdened, often humorless* — not a chipper pally with a warhorse and a hi-yo attitude.

## When Stuck

Ask: *what did this place lose, and who still carries it?*
