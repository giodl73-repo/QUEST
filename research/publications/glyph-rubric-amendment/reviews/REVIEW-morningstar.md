# Review: Innovation-Cluster-Driven Rubric Amendment: Evidence-Based Evolution of a Playtest Scoring System
**Reviewer:** Jason Morningstar, Bully Pulpit Games
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** tabletop RPG design, playtesting, narrative structure

## Summary
From a practitioner standpoint, this paper describes something game designers do informally and constantly — noticing when a session breaks the model you were using and updating your model. What is novel here is the systematic pipeline: innovations are logged, clusters are detected, amendments are made with explicit rationale, and the effect is measured. This is not how playtesting works in the industry, but it is how it should work. The 13-version history over 120+ innovations is an extraordinary record of design learning in action.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Comparison to alternative amendment methods**: The paper would benefit from a comparison to how practitioners actually amend their playtest rubrics — typically informal, undocumented, and driven by the loudest complaint rather than systematic cluster detection. Even a qualitative comparison to practitioner norms would help readers understand what the pipeline adds.

## Minor Issues
- **Threshold justification**: The 3-innovation threshold is presented as a design decision but not justified. From a practitioner perspective, I could imagine arguing for 2 (more responsive) or 5 (more conservative). The paper should at least discuss what the threshold is trading off between.
- **Decay pattern visualization**: The observation that innovations cluster at archetype-burst (campaign onset) and decay as the system adapts is the most interesting empirical finding in the paper. It needs a dedicated figure.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
Strong. The practitioner audience will recognize the problem immediately. The academic framing for NeurIPS D&B is handled gracefully.

### Methodology
The pipeline is described with enough detail for a practitioner to adapt it. The innovation log format (dimension, session, description, cluster assignment) is clearly specified. This is good technical writing.

### Evaluation
The 13-version amendment timeline is the paper's empirical centerpiece. I would like to see the version transitions annotated with the specific design decisions they enabled downstream — did v1.3's addition of the Act-Without-Announcement dimension enable the detection of player style patterns that became a separate paper? If so, that is a contribution worth naming explicitly.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper is a genuine contribution to systematic playtesting methodology. The threshold and comparison issues are the main revision items. The venue question (NeurIPS D&B vs. FDG) is worth revisiting — this paper would be received warmly at FDG and would have an easier review process there.
