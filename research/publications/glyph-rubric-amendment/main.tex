\begin{abstract}
Scoring rubrics for creative tasks face a fundamental tension: they must be
specific enough to be operationally useful, but creative practice continually
produces outcomes that fall outside any finite set of anchors. We describe a
mechanism for resolving this tension: \textit{innovation-cluster-driven rubric
amendment}, in which session-level surprises are logged, clustered by dimension,
and promoted to rubric amendments when 2 or more cluster in the same dimension.
Applied across 49 AI-simulated Dungeons \& Dragons sessions in 7 complete
campaigns, the mechanism produced 120+ logged innovation entries and 13 rubric
versions over a period of 4 days. Amendments cluster thematically: Campaign 4
(siege-resource archetype) produced 16 innovations that drove 4 amendments
(v1.9--v1.12) addressing grief-paragraph situational resonance, sustained
behavioral pattern as agency, character-register conversion, and
Portent-as-directed-foreknowledge. The mechanism is self-calibrating:
early sessions produce many innovations (12 in Session 1), later sessions fewer
(2--4 per session), as the rubric absorbs common patterns, while novel archetypes
(Campaigns 5--7, each with new professional-code, duty-spine, and no-stakes
designs) produce new innovation bursts. We argue this pipeline --- log,
cluster, threshold, amend, forward-only --- is a replicable methodology for any
scoring system applied to creative evaluation in a production context.
\end{abstract}
