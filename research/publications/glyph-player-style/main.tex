\begin{abstract}
Describing player behavior in tabletop role-playing games (TRPGs) systematically is
difficult: sessions are unrecorded, behavior is attributed to player personality rather
than character-sheet design, and no formal mechanism exists for promoting session
surprises into reusable design patterns. We describe a pipeline that addresses this
gap, applied across 49 AI-simulated Dungeons \& Dragons 5e sessions spanning 7
complete campaigns. The pipeline promotes behavioral surprises to ratified
\textit{player styles} when 3 or more instances cluster thematically across 2 or more
sessions. Seven styles were promoted: \textit{sheet-deep-reader} (\char`\~50 instances),
\textit{craft-witness} (\char`\~45 instances), \textit{npc-arc-completion trigger}
(\char`\~40 instances), \textit{act-without-announcement} (\char`\~25 instances),
\textit{verbal-documentation-arc} (\char`\~15 instances), \textit{documentary-witness}
(\char`\~12 instances), and \textit{act-without-announcement intra-session} (\char`\~8 instances).
Cross-campaign validation against 12 research hypotheses (RV1--RV12) confirms that all
7 styles fire across all 7 campaigns and are not party-archetype-specific. Four
PC-authored finale deliverables across 4 campaigns demonstrate that player styles
accumulate toward campaign-completing acts when character-sheet design enables it.
\end{abstract}
