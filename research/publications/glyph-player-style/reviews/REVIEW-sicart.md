# Review: Emergent Player Styles in AI-Simulated TRPG: From Innovation Instances to Ratified Design Patterns
**Reviewer:** Miguel Sicart, IT University of Copenhagen
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** game philosophy, play theory, ethics of game design

## Summary
This paper applies an empirical pipeline to a domain — player behavior in AI-simulated TRPG — that has not been systematically studied. The resulting taxonomy of seven styles is interesting, particularly the act-without-announcement and craft-witness styles, which reflect a kind of aesthetic restraint that prior player-type models do not capture well. The cross-archetype validation is the paper's strongest result. The paper needs grounding in design theory and a comparison to prior player-type frameworks to fully realize its contribution.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Promotion threshold needs design theory grounding**: The 3/2 threshold is a classification decision with theoretical implications. In play theory terms, is 3 instances the threshold at which a behavior becomes a "play style" rather than an "idiosyncratic act"? The paper should engage with this question — even briefly — to give the threshold a theoretical basis rather than leaving it as an engineering choice.

## Minor Issues
- **Bartle/Yee comparison**: Required for any player-type taxonomy paper. The act-without-announcement style in particular seems to map partially to Yee's "Escapism" and "Immersion" motivators — a comparison would clarify the relationship.
- **Play theory grounding**: Styles like "craft-witness" and "verbal-documentation-arc" are about reflexivity and documentation, not just play performance. These styles raise interesting questions about the relationship between playing and witnessing/recording play. A brief connection to play theory (Suits, Bogost, or Sicart's own work on play as a form of expression) would deepen the paper's theoretical contribution.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The paper opens with the right question — what behavioral patterns emerge from AI-simulated TRPG sessions that the rubric did not anticipate? — and it should stay closer to that question throughout. The innovation-to-style pipeline is the paper's methodological heart.

### Methodology
The RV1–RV12 systematic annotation is described clearly. The paper should clarify whether innovation identification was conducted by a single analyst or multiple analysts, and whether the style classifications were verified by anyone other than the original annotator.

### Evaluation
The seven styles are individually described but not collectively analyzed. A figure showing the inter-relationships between styles — which styles tend to co-occur in the same session? in the same PC? — would make the taxonomy more analytically useful.

## Recommendation
Accept with revisions. The threshold grounding and Bartle/Yee comparison are required. The play theory connection is optional but would strengthen the paper's contribution claim. FDG is the right venue.
