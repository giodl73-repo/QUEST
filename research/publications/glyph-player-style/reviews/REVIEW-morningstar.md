# Review: Emergent Player Styles in AI-Simulated TRPG: From Innovation Instances to Ratified Design Patterns
**Reviewer:** Jason Morningstar, Bully Pulpit Games
**Score:** 4 — strong accept
**Expertise:** tabletop RPG design, playtesting, narrative structure

## Summary
From a designer's perspective, this paper is exactly what I want from games research: a taxonomy of player behaviors that emerged from play, not from a priori theorizing, with enough corpus evidence to be practically actionable. The seven styles are recognizable — I have seen act-without-announcement players at my tables; I have seen craft-witness players; I have seen verbal-documentation players. The fact that these styles are archetype-independent (they emerge across grief-themed parties, compact-wardens, and the-witnesses) is the finding that makes this practically useful rather than corpus-specific.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Threshold justification**: The 3/2 threshold should be explained, even briefly. A design rationale (e.g., "we chose 3 as a minimum cluster size because 1–2 instances are insufficient to distinguish a style from an individual player habit") is sufficient for FDG. A sensitivity analysis would be better.

## Minor Issues
- **Act-without-announcement variants**: The v1.5 and v1.12 variants of act-without-announcement are meaningfully distinct in the session logs — one is about restraint, one is about action. The paper should make this distinction explicit. Practitioners need to know whether these are the same style with contextual variation or genuinely different behavioral patterns.
- **4 PC-authored deliverables**: This is mentioned as a finding but not given adequate prominence. PC-authored deliverables (Kessa's commonplace book entry, Thera's bread-cutting) are the thing a designer most wants to know: are players producing artifacts that survive the session? This finding deserves its own subsection.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
Strong. The innovation-to-style pipeline framing is clear. The paper should open with a concrete example of a player style in action before the abstract description — FDG readers respond well to narrative grounding.

### Methodology
The RV1–RV12 systematic annotation is impressive and gives me confidence in the taxonomy's evidence base. I would like to see the full annotation key as an appendix.

### Evaluation
The per-style cross-archetype evidence is the paper's strongest section. The PC-authored deliverables finding should be pulled into the evaluation as a named result, not left as a count in the methodology.

## Recommendation
Strong accept. This is a genuinely useful contribution to game design practice and games research. The threshold and variant distinction are minor issues that should be addressed in revision but do not block publication.
