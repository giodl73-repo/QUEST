# Review: Emergent Player Styles in AI-Simulated TRPG: From Innovation Instances to Ratified Design Patterns
**Reviewer:** Evan Torner, University of Cincinnati
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** LARP studies, game studies methods, Nordic freeform

## Summary
Player-type taxonomies are a well-worn but persistently useful topic in game studies, and this paper's contribution is the methodology — detecting styles from a session innovation log rather than from player self-reports or designer intent. The seven resulting styles are plausible and the cross-archetype validation is solid. The comparison to Bartle and Yee is missing and should be included; the threshold justification is the paper's main methodological gap.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Promotion threshold justification**: The 3/2 threshold is the classification criterion and it is not justified. Either a sensitivity analysis or a principled design rationale is required. This is the paper's primary P1 item.

## Minor Issues
- **Bartle/Yee comparison required**: The paper proposes a player-style taxonomy and does not cite Bartle's four types or Yee's Motivational Model. This is a gap that will be noticed by every FDG reviewer. The paper does not need to claim these prior frameworks are wrong — it needs to explain how AI-simulated TRPG styles relate to them (overlap, divergence, or orthogonality).
- **Design theory grounding**: The paper identifies styles empirically but does not connect them to design theory. Which of the seven styles are facilitated by the adventure design? Which emerge despite it? A brief analysis of the design conditions that enable each style would make the taxonomy actionable for designers.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction is clear but could be strengthened by explicitly situating the contribution as a methodology paper as well as a taxonomy paper. The pipeline (innovation instance → cluster → ratified style) is as much the contribution as the seven resulting styles.

### Methodology
The promotion pipeline is described in prose but would benefit from a formal diagram. The distinction between session-level and cross-session threshold conditions (3 per session OR 2 across 2+ sessions) is clear in prose but would be more legible as a decision tree or flowchart.

### Evaluation
The cross-archetype validation table is the paper's best evidence. I would like to see per-style instance counts per campaign alongside the binary "appears/does not appear" indicator currently shown.

## Recommendation
Accept with revisions. The threshold justification is required; the Bartle/Yee comparison is required. Both are manageable. The paper is a solid FDG contribution and should be published after revision.
