\begin{abstract}
We compare two resource exhaustion mechanics against five campaigns with no degrading-resource
mechanics, using Character Agency (CA) scores from the Marathon D\&D Workshop as the primary
outcome. Campaign 4's Supply Die (d12 degrading to exhausted over 7 sessions, tracking garrison
healing supplies) produced a \textbf{+0.6 CA-point advantage} over light-pressure baselines at
sessions 1--6, reaching the rubric's CA 10 ceiling by session 3 --- three sessions earlier than
comparable no-resource campaigns. Campaign 6's Unit Cohesion Die (d12 degrading to d8 as named
unit members were lost) matched the Supply Die's gate score trajectory while producing an
additional \textbf{+3.0 panel-session advantage} in sessions 5--7: three consecutive sessions
rated \textit{character-primary} by the five-lens panel, versus \textit{character-inflected
tactical} for the Supply Die across those same sessions. Both resource mechanics produced
monotone-rising CA trajectories (7$\to$8$\to$10$\to$10$\to$10$\to$10$\to$10 and
7$\to$8$\to$9$\to$10$\to$10$\to$10$\to$10 respectively), while all five non-resource
campaigns showed stable or late-rising trajectories reaching CA 10 only at or near the finale.
We argue that resource exhaustion amplifies character-driven decision-making --- rather than
suppressing it --- when two conditions hold: the resource is \textit{legible} (depletion is
directly observable) and \textit{emotionally architectured} (the resource represents something
the party has reason to care about beyond tactical survival). The Unit Cohesion Die's stronger
advantage is explained by its tracking of named people rather than materials: the party
preserved the Cohesion resource because they cared about the people it represented, not
because preservation was tactically optimal. Limits of the analysis (two campaigns, single
system, AI-simulated play, archetype confound) are discussed.
\end{abstract}
