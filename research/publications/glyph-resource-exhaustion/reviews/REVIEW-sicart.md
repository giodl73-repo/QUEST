# Review: Resource Exhaustion as Narrative Amplifier: Mechanical Attrition in Long-Form Tabletop RPG
**Reviewer:** Miguel Sicart, IT University of Copenhagen
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** game philosophy, play theory, ethics of game design

## Summary
The paper advances a claim that has been intuited by TRPG designers for decades but rarely studied systematically: that running out of resources creates narrative meaning. The +0.6 and +3.0 CA results are a starting point, and the unit-cohesion mechanism is the paper's most compelling theoretical contribution. The archetype confound is real but the paper's framing as a FDG contribution means it can be honestly addressed as a limitation without blocking publication.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Archetype confound**: The military and craft-witness party archetypes in the two resource-exhaustion campaigns are both predisposed to high unit-cohesion and narrative depth by archetype design. The paper needs to address this confound more directly — either by arguing that the CA gain exceeds what archetype alone would predict, or by reframing as a hypothesis paper that identifies this as a confound requiring controlled replication.

## Minor Issues
- **Character Agency as rubric-internal outcome**: The CA score is an internal measure. An independent operationalization — or at minimum a comparison of CA score behavior in non-resource campaigns of similar archetypes — would strengthen the claim.
- **Published resource-exhaustion game design comparison**: Torchbearer, Ironsworn, and Burning Wheel all use resource exhaustion as a narrative amplifier by design intent. A brief comparison of those games' design rationales with this paper's empirical findings would locate the contribution and add credibility.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction frames the paper's claim clearly. The connection to ethics-of-game-design literature (player experience of meaningful struggle) would be appropriate here and would give the paper a stronger theoretical foundation than proceduralism alone.

### Methodology
The paper should specify the resource-exhaustion mechanic precisely: what resources are tracked, what depletion conditions look like mechanically, and how this differs from standard TRPG resource management. The distinction between "resource exhaustion" and "normal attrition" needs to be crisply defined.

### Evaluation
The +0.6 vs. +3.0 differential is interesting. The paper should ask: what happened in C6 that did not happen in C4 that produced such a large difference? If the answer is archetype or adventure design, that is important to know. If the answer is accumulated resource pressure across more sessions, that is a finding about the mechanic's longitudinal effect.

## Recommendation
Accept with revisions. The archetype confound should be addressed more directly in the revision — either as a controlled analysis or as an acknowledged limitation with a clear replication study design. The paper is a solid FDG contribution with a genuine hypothesis worth pursuing.
