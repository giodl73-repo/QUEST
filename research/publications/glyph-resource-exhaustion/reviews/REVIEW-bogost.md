# Review: Resource Exhaustion as Narrative Amplifier: Mechanical Attrition in Long-Form Tabletop RPG
**Reviewer:** Ian Bogost, Georgia Tech / Washington University in St. Louis
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** game criticism, procedural rhetoric

## Summary
This paper makes an interesting argument that resource exhaustion — tracking what players have spent and what they lack — amplifies narrative engagement in ways that non-resource-exhaustion campaigns do not produce. The C4 (+0.6 CA) and C6 (+3.0 CA) findings are a starting point, though the small n and archetype confound limit what can be inferred. The paper's most interesting claim is the mechanism: unit-cohesion is stronger in resource-exhaustion campaigns because shared attrition creates shared narrative stakes. This is a proceduralist argument (Bogost 2006) and I find it compelling in principle; it needs more empirical support.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Archetype confound**: Campaign 4 is a military party; Campaign 6 is a craft-witness/unit-cohesion party. Both are resource-exhaustion campaigns. The Character Agency gain in these campaigns may reflect archetype properties (military parties track unit-cohesion by design; craft-witness parties amplify narrative depth) rather than the resource-exhaustion mechanic. The paper cannot attribute the CA gain to resource exhaustion without controlling for archetype.

## Minor Issues
- **n=2 resource campaigns**: Two resource campaigns is not a corpus; it is a pair of observations. The paper should frame its contribution as a hypothesis paper (resource exhaustion as a mechanism worth studying) rather than an evidence paper (resource exhaustion produces measurable narrative amplification). The framing should be revised throughout.
- **Comparison to published resource-exhaustion game designs**: The paper does not compare its findings to published games that use resource exhaustion as a design principle (Torchbearer, Ironsworn, 13th Age's Escalation Die). Even a qualitative comparison would locate the contribution.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction's proceduralist framing is appropriate. The paper should cite prior proceduralist theory more explicitly — the argument that mechanics shape narrative meaning is well-established and the paper benefits from connecting to it.

### Methodology
The Character Agency score is described as the primary outcome measure. The paper should specify how Character Agency is calculated (rubric dimension, anchors, possible range) and why it is the appropriate outcome measure for testing the resource-exhaustion hypothesis.

### Evaluation
The +0.6 vs. +3.0 CA differential is the paper's most striking result. The paper should discuss whether the differential is attributable to adventure design (C6 was designed with the resource mechanic in mind from the start; C4 was adapted) rather than the mechanic itself.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper is a FDG contribution, not a definitive empirical result. The archetype confound and n=2 are real limitations that should be more prominent. The hypothesis paper framing is the appropriate revision.
