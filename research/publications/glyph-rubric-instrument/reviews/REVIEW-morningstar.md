# Review: An 8-Dimension Playtest Rubric as a Research Instrument for AI-Simulated Narrative Game Sessions
**Reviewer:** Jason Morningstar, Bully Pulpit Games
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** tabletop RPG design, playtesting, narrative structure

## Summary
As a TRPG designer, I read this paper with professional interest and came away impressed by the seriousness with which the authors have approached the question of what a playtest actually measures. The 8 dimensions read as coherent to a practitioner — they map to things I actually care about at the table. The 49-session corpus is genuinely large for systematic TRPG research. The amendment pipeline, particularly the innovation-cluster-to-rubric-amendment pathway, is something I wish I'd had when iterating on our own design tools.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Inter-rater reliability**: From a practitioner standpoint, if I were adopting this rubric for my own playtests, I would want to know whether two people using it would score the same session similarly. This is not an academic formality — it is a practical question about whether the rubric is a shared language or a personal idiom. The paper should address this.

## Minor Issues
- **Comparison to existing playtest tools**: The paper does not compare to any existing TRPG playtesting practice or framework. Bully Pulpit's own playtesting process, the Gauntlet's feedback protocol, or Pelgrane's session feedback forms would all be meaningful comparisons — not to claim superiority but to locate the contribution.
- **What does 77 mean?**: The paper reports a mean gate of 77 in the final corpus epoch without context for what 77 represents qualitatively. A brief mapping of scores to session quality descriptors (e.g., "sessions scoring above 80 consistently produced emotionally memorable moments") would ground the number.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
Strong. The framing of the rubric as "the instrument, not just the outcome" is the paper's best sentence. Keep it prominent.

### Methodology
The session pipeline (PREP → PLAY → LOG → session-gate → playtest-panel → playtest-innovation → session-handoff) is described with useful clarity. The single-AI-system limitation is real but not fatal for a CHI contribution — the authors should be more direct about what the AI system is and what its known behavioral characteristics are, so readers can calibrate generalizability.

### Evaluation
The dimension-level breakdown across sessions is this paper's most practically useful content. A TRPG designer reading this can directly map the amendment history to design lessons. I would like to see one more dimension of evidence: which amendment had the largest positive impact on mean gate, and why?

## Recommendation
Accept. This is a useful, well-grounded contribution to both the games research community and practitioners. The inter-rater reliability gap is the main revision priority. The paper is ready for publication once that is addressed — either through a study or through an honest reframing of what "research instrument" means in the absence of that data.
