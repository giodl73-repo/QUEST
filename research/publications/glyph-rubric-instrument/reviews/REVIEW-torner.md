# Review: An 8-Dimension Playtest Rubric as a Research Instrument for AI-Simulated Narrative Game Sessions
**Reviewer:** Evan Torner, University of Cincinnati
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** LARP studies, game studies methods, Nordic freeform

## Summary
This paper occupies a useful and underserved position between game design practice and HCI research methods. The rubric's 8 dimensions are well-motivated and show evidence of evolution through actual play — a quality too few game evaluation instruments can claim. The 49-session corpus spanning three campaigns with different party archetypes is strong evidence for robustness. The paper's main theoretical contribution — that the rubric-as-instrument should be treated as a dynamic artifact rather than a fixed scale — has implications beyond TRPG research.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Inter-rater reliability absent**: Game studies methods scholars will immediately ask about inter-rater agreement. The paper's claim to be offering a "research instrument" is undermined without this. At minimum, a second rater should score a 10-session sample, and the agreement should be reported and discussed. If agreement is reasonable, this strengthens the paper significantly. If it is poor, that is itself an interesting finding about what these sessions require from an evaluator.

## Minor Issues
- **Dragonlance setting limitation**: The paper's single-setting constraint is acknowledged but not sufficiently theorized. Which of the 8 dimensions are setting-agnostic (e.g., Atmospheric Landing) versus setting-inflected (e.g., Lore Integration)? A brief taxonomy would improve transferability claims.
- **Comparison to existing playtest tools**: A minimal engagement with prior systematic playtesting research (even one or two citations) would locate the contribution more precisely.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction does good work framing the problem. The authors should consider adding a one-paragraph "guide to the paper" at the end of the introduction — CHI readers benefit from explicit signposting of what is empirical versus what is design contribution.

### Methodology
The amendment pipeline is presented clearly. The paper should include a table or timeline of all rubric versions, with the triggering innovation cluster noted for each amendment. This is currently fragmented across sections and would be more useful as a consolidated artifact.

### Evaluation
The cross-campaign validation is presented convincingly. The decision to include three distinct party archetypes (grief-themed, compact-wardens, and the-witnesses) is methodologically sound and should be made more prominent in the abstract and introduction — it is a key claim.

## Recommendation
Accept with revisions. The inter-rater reliability gap is the critical revision item. The paper is otherwise a sound contribution and should be published at CHI after addressing this gap, either through a study or through a carefully argued reframing of the instrument's current validation state.
