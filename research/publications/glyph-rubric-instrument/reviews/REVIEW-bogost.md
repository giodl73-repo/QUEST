# Review: An 8-Dimension Playtest Rubric as a Research Instrument for AI-Simulated Narrative Game Sessions
**Reviewer:** Ian Bogost, Georgia Tech / Washington University in St. Louis
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** game criticism, procedural rhetoric

## Summary
This paper presents a thoughtful contribution to the thin literature on systematic evaluation of AI-generated tabletop RPG sessions. The 49-session corpus is genuinely impressive for this domain, and the amendment pipeline — treating the rubric as a living instrument rather than a fixed instrument — is a conceptually interesting move that game studies researchers should find compelling. The mean gate improvement from 65 to 77 is presented with appropriate hedging, though more could be done to contextualize what these scores mean outside the system's own logic.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Inter-rater reliability absent**: The paper has a single scorer applying the rubric across all 49 sessions. Without a second independent coder on a sample (even 10 sessions), readers cannot assess whether the 8-dimension scores reflect something stable about the sessions or the scorer's interpretive habits. This must be addressed — it is the paper's central methodological gap.

## Minor Issues
- **Comparison to existing playtest tools**: The paper does not situate itself against published playtesting frameworks (e.g., Fullerton's game design workshop protocols, Dana Ruggiero's GBL rubrics). Even a one-paragraph acknowledgment of the landscape would strengthen the contribution claim.
- **Gate score as absolute vs. relative measure**: "65 to 77" reads as progress, but the paper would benefit from a brief argument about whether 77 is good, adequate, or merely better.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The framing around "rubric as research instrument" is the paper's strongest theoretical move. The authors should lean harder into this in the introduction — specifically, the claim that a rubric can be both the design artifact and the measurement instrument is non-obvious and deserves a more explicit defense against the circularity concern.

### Methodology
The 49-session corpus spanning 3 campaigns is the paper's empirical foundation. The session sampling description is clear. However, the single-scorer limitation is not acknowledged as a limitation in the methodology section — it appears only implicitly in the threats-to-validity section. Move this forward; do not bury it.

### Evaluation
The mean gate improvement graph is readable. The per-campaign breakdown is useful. A dimension-level breakdown of where sessions most improved (and which dimensions remained flat) would strengthen the evidence that the amendment pipeline is doing real work.

## Recommendation
Accept. The corpus size, the systematic amendment pipeline, and the cross-campaign validation are genuine contributions. The inter-rater reliability gap is real but addressable through revision; it should not block publication at CHI where field-building papers routinely face this methodological frontier. Revision required on inter-rater reliability framing and comparison-to-existing-tools gap.
