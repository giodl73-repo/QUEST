%%% ─── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Measuring the quality of AI-simulated tabletop role-playing game sessions is an
unsolved problem: when no human players participate, there are no exit surveys, no
post-session interviews, and no participant accounts of what was experienced. We
present the Marathon Playtest Rubric, an 8-dimension instrument covering Engagement,
Mechanical Fairness, Pacing, Character Agency, Module Fidelity, Atmospheric Landing,
Surprise, and Table Readiness, each scored 0--10 for a maximum of 80 points per
session. We validate the rubric across 49 AI-simulated Dungeons \& Dragons 5th
Edition sessions spanning 7 complete campaigns with distinct party archetypes and
central dramatic questions. Mean gate scores improved from 65/80 at campaign
inception (rubric v1.0, sessions 1--2) to 77/80 at campaign maturity (v1.12,
sessions 43--49), a 12-point improvement driven by 13 incremental amendments. The
three most recent campaigns score within 1.4 points of the four earliest campaigns,
demonstrating that quality measurements are consistent across campaign archetype and
theme. The rubric's amendment pipeline --- innovations logged, clustered, and
codified as new anchors --- is itself characterized as a learning mechanism applicable
beyond TRPG research.
\end{abstract}
