# Review: The 7-Session Campaign Spine: Evidence-Based Architecture for Emotionally Complete Narrative Arcs
**Reviewer:** Ian Bogost, Georgia Tech / Washington University in St. Louis
**Score:** 2 — weak accept
**Expertise:** game criticism, procedural rhetoric

## Summary
The paper presents a design template for long-form narrative arcs in AI-simulated TRPG and argues it is "evidence-based" on the strength of three campaigns achieving 7/7 completion. I find the template interesting and the 5/7 Route D/E finding significant. However, the paper's core concept — "emotional completeness" — is not defined outside of the rubric scores that the spine was designed to optimize. This is not merely a methodological quibble; it is a conceptual gap. "Emotionally complete" is doing a lot of work in the abstract, the title, and the conclusion, and the paper does not tell me what that phrase means in terms I can apply independently.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **"Emotional completeness" operationalization**: The paper owes the reader a definition of emotional completeness that is independent of the rubric scores. This is the paper's central claim and it is currently supported only by circular evidence. The fix is theoretical: ground emotional completeness in prior narrative theory (Murray, Aristotle, Chatman, Bal) and then show that the spine produces sessions that meet those criteria. Or: use a human panel to rate transcripts for "narrative completeness" without rubric context and show correlation with rubric scores.

## Minor Issues
- **n=7 from the same author**: Three campaigns, seven sessions each, one author-evaluator. The paper cannot generalize from this corpus without acknowledging the confound. The same author who designed the spine scored the sessions — there is no independent evaluation of whether a different designer using the spine would achieve comparable results.
- **Comparison to published campaign frameworks**: I would like to know how the 7-session spine compares to Pathfinder Adventure Paths (6 modules), D&D's published campaign arcs, or academic studies of serialized narrative structure.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction overclaims in the abstract. "Emotionally complete narrative arcs" as a validated outcome is the paper's conclusion, not its premise — the introduction should be more careful about framing the claim as "arcs that score highly on an emotional dimension rubric" until the operationalization is established.

### Methodology
The spine structure (7 adventures; PC spotlight; 4 hints planted across sessions; Route A–E outcome taxonomy) is clearly described. The hint-system is the paper's most technically novel element and should be given more space.

### Evaluation
The three-campaign comparison table is useful. The paper should add a "failure mode" analysis: what would a campaign look like if it used the spine and still failed to achieve completion? Specifying failure modes would bound the spine's generalizability claims.

## Recommendation
Weak accept — revise. The emotional completeness operationalization is required before camera-ready. The same-author confound should be acknowledged more substantively. Both are manageable in a revision cycle.
