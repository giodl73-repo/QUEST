# Review Synthesis: The 7-Session Campaign Spine: Evidence-Based Architecture for Emotionally Complete Narrative Arcs

**Venue:** CHI 2027
**Mean Score:** 2.8 (Murray 3, Riedl 3, Bogost 2, Bernstein 2, Morningstar 4)
**Recommendation:** Revise and Resubmit

---

## P1 — Priority 1 (3+ reviewers, blocking)

**Operationalize "emotional completeness" independently of the rubric score.**
All five reviewers cited this. Bogost and Bernstein are weak accepts (score 2) specifically because of this gap. Murray and Riedl accept conditionally on it. The term "emotional completeness" appears in the title, abstract, and conclusion without a definition that is independent of the rubric scores the spine was designed to produce.

- **Required fix (theoretical path)**: Ground "emotional completeness" in prior narrative theory. Candidates: Aristotle's dramatic catharsis, Murray's four pleasures of cyberdrama (immersion, agency, transformation, narrative expectation), Chatman's story/discourse distinction, Labov's narrative clauses. Choose 1–2 and argue that the spine produces sessions that meet these criteria. Show the argument is not simply "high rubric score = emotionally complete."

- **Required fix (empirical path)**: Conduct a blind rater study. Have 5–10 readers rate session transcripts on "narrative completeness" or "emotional satisfaction" without rubric context. Compute correlation with rubric scores. If correlation is high, this validates the rubric as a proxy. If low, the paper must revise its claims.

- **Minimum acceptable fix**: Add an explicit theoretical definition of "emotional completeness" in Section 2, derived from narrative theory, applied to each of the three campaigns with specific examples of how the sessions meet the definition. Acknowledge that the rubric is a practical operationalization of this theoretical definition, not its source.

---

## P2 — Priority 2 (2+ reviewers, required for acceptance)

**Address the same-author confound.**
Bogost and Bernstein both named this. Four reviewers acknowledged it. Three campaigns from one author-evaluator means the spine's success may reflect the author's design skill rather than the spine's architectural properties.

- **Required fix (reframing path)**: Reframe the contribution as "one author's evidence-based design practice." This is honest and still valuable. Add a replication protocol section: what would a second designer need to know to run a campaign using this spine? What are the necessary and sufficient structural conditions?
- **Required fix (expansion path)**: If a second-author replication is feasible before the revision deadline, add it. Even one campaign by a different designer would substantially strengthen the generalizability claim.

---

## P3 — Advisory (1–2 reviewers, strengthens but does not block)

**Compare against published campaign design frameworks.**
Murray, Riedl, Bogost, and Bernstein all asked for this. Pathfinder Adventure Paths, D&D's published campaign design guidelines, Matt Colville's structure guide, and academic studies of serialized narrative are the relevant comparands. A qualitative comparison (1–2 paragraphs) is sufficient.

---

## Convergent Strengths (Do Not Revise Away)

- 7/7 completion rate across three campaigns: all five reviewers cited this. Protect it prominently.
- 5/7 Route D/E player-authored resolution: Morningstar called this the headline; he is right. Make it the paper's lead result.
- Archetype-independence across grief-themed, compact-wardens, the-witnesses: Riedl and Murray both noted this as important evidence. Keep it prominent.
- 4-hint system and PC spotlight rotation: Morningstar and Murray both called these the spine's most novel elements. Give them more space.

---

## Divergent Notes

- Morningstar (strong accept, 4) provides strong practitioner endorsement. Bogost and Bernstein (weak accept, 2) are the academic skeptics. The gap is specifically the emotional completeness operationalization and same-author confound — both are real and addressable.
- Murray and Riedl (accept, 3) are conditionally favorable and will move to accept if P1 is addressed.
- The paper is viable at CHI once P1 and P2 are addressed. Without these revisions, the paper is at risk from Bogost and Bernstein.
