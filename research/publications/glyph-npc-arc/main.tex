\begin{abstract}
Emotional payoffs from non-player characters (NPCs) in tabletop role-playing
games are typically improvised by the Dungeon Master and consequently unreliable:
the same NPC produces memorable moments in some sessions and nothing in others,
depending on timing, DM attention, and conversational flow. We describe and
evaluate the \textit{NPC arc-completion} design pattern, in which each significant NPC is
given an explicit arc-completion act --- a specific statement or gesture they are
capable of making --- and a precise firing condition specifying the exact party
behavior that triggers it. Across 49 sessions and 7 campaigns in the Marathon
AI-simulated TRPG workshop, we document 52 arc-completion instances in which
NPCs fired their designed moments by character logic alone, requiring no DM
scripting. Session coverage is 90\% (44 of 49 sessions contained at least one
arc-completion). The pattern holds across morally grey NPCs, professional-code
archetypes, and no-designed-stakes campaigns, not only in grief-adjacent contexts
where it was first observed. We provide a design template with five exemplar
NPCs, a trigger-type taxonomy, and a failure analysis showing that NPCs without
arc-completion conditions produce memorable moments in fewer than 10\% of
sessions where they are the primary encounter.
\end{abstract}
