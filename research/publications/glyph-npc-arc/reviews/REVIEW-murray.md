# Review: Designing NPC Arc-Completion Conditions for Session-Invariant Emotional Payoffs in AI-Simulated TRPG
**Reviewer:** Janet Murray, Georgia Tech Digital Media
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** interactive narrative, cyberdrama, digital storytelling

## Summary
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to interactive narrative design by formalizing what practitioners call "NPC arcs" as designable artifacts with specifiable completion conditions. The 40+ instance corpus, 88% session coverage, and zero-DM-scripted payoff rate are strong evidence that the design template is doing real work. The core theoretical move — distinguishing "character logic" from "DM scripting" as the driver of arc-completion — is important and has implications beyond TRPGs for any system that aims to generate emotionally resonant NPC behavior.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Drama management comparison is thin**: The paper gestures at drama management (Riedl, Young, Louchart & Aylett) as a foil but does not engage substantively. If the claim is that character-logic-driven arc-completion outperforms or is distinct from drama management approaches, that argument needs to be made carefully with citations and a clear theoretical distinction.

## Minor Issues
- **No player studies**: The paper's evidence is entirely rubric-internal — sessions scored well, NPC arcs fired, DM did not script them. Whether players (human or AI-simulated) actually found these moments emotionally memorable is not independently assessed. This is acknowledged in the paper but understated.
- **"Memorable payoff" operationalization**: The paper uses rubric scores as a proxy for "memorable payoff." These are correlated by construction, not by independent evidence. A brief argument for why high rubric scores are a reasonable proxy — or an acknowledgment that this circularity is a limitation — would strengthen the paper.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction's framing around "session-invariance" is the paper's strongest theoretical contribution. The claim that a well-designed NPC arc will fire across different session contexts (different party compositions, different encounter routes) is non-obvious and the paper provides evidence for it. This should be front-loaded more aggressively.

### Methodology
The arc-completion design template is the paper's practical contribution and is clearly specified. The "emotional position + completion condition + triggering context" three-part structure is elegant and teachable. The paper should include one worked example of an NPC arc template alongside the abstract description — practitioners will want to see what the template looks like instantiated.

### Evaluation
The 40+ instance corpus is solid. The 88% session coverage figure is the paper's headline result and should be accompanied by a breakdown: which sessions did NPC arcs NOT fire in, and why? Identifying failure cases is more informative than the success rate alone.

## Recommendation
Accept with minor revisions. The drama management comparison is the key revision item. The paper is otherwise a strong CHI contribution with clear practical value for interactive narrative designers.
