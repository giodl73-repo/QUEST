# Review: Designing NPC Arc-Completion Conditions for Session-Invariant Emotional Payoffs in AI-Simulated TRPG
**Reviewer:** Ian Bogost, Georgia Tech / Washington University in St. Louis
**Score:** 3 — accept
**Expertise:** game criticism, procedural rhetoric

## Summary
This paper formalizes something that game designers do intuitively — giving NPCs an emotional endpoint and trusting that reaching it will create a payoff — and asks whether it can be systematized into a designable template. The answer, based on 40+ instances across three campaigns, appears to be yes. I find the character-logic framing theoretically interesting, though underdeveloped. The paper would benefit from a stronger engagement with drama management as the closest prior art.

## Major Issues (P1 if 3+ reviewers or blocking)
- **Drama management comparison thin**: The paper's central claim — that character-logic is the right driver for arc-completion, not scripted DM intervention — is a claim about what kind of computational approach is preferable. This claim needs to be argued against drama management theory, not just gestured at. The paper should spend at least a half-page on why drama management's approach to NPC resolution differs from and falls short of what the character-logic template achieves.

## Minor Issues
- **"Memorable payoff" is rubric-internal**: The circularity between rubric design and payoff measurement is the paper's main methodological vulnerability. The authors should address this more directly — either by acknowledging the circularity as a limitation or by providing an independent operationalization.
- **Session-invariance claim needs sharper definition**: "Session-invariant" is the paper's key adjective but it is not formally defined. Does it mean the arc fires regardless of party composition? Regardless of encounter route? Regardless of session length? A brief formal definition would make the claim testable.

## Detailed Comments
### Introduction
The introduction could be sharpened by opening with the problem (NPCs that feel scripted undermine emotional investment) rather than the solution. The current opening reads as a design document introduction rather than a research paper introduction.

### Methodology
The three-part template is clearly specified and I can imagine a game designer using it. The paper should include the full NPC arc specifications for 2–3 canonical instances from the corpus — not as appendix material, but in the body. This would make the template's practical application visible to readers.

### Evaluation
The 40+ instances are presented as a count but not as a distribution. A histogram of arc-completion timing (which scene within a session, which session within a campaign) would reveal whether arcs are front-loaded, back-loaded, or evenly distributed — and whether that distribution is a design outcome or an emergent property.

## Recommendation
Accept with revisions. The drama management comparison is the required revision. The paper is ready for CHI once that theoretical gap is addressed.
