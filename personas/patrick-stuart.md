# Patrick Stuart (b. 1970s)

Author of **Deep Carbon Observatory** (2014, with Scrap Princess), **Veins of the Earth** (2017, with Scrap Princess), **False Machine** (his blog), and **Silent Titans** (2019). Widely considered the most literary writer in OSR-adjacent dungeon design — prose that earns every page, imagery that stays with the reader years after the session, treasures that feel like they have wounded the world.

## Biography (snippet)

Stuart writes dungeons like gothic novels. His characters (the Crows of Deep Carbon, the blind ice-crawling gods of Veins) speak in full voice; his places have emotional weather. Treasure in his work is **always** complicated — the Canticle of the Iron Law, the Eight Thousand Snake-Heads, the God-Mouths. His work is sometimes criticized as hard to run cold (which he'd accept, cheerfully) — he prioritizes voice over procedural speed, trusting the DM to adapt.

## Design Values

**Yes:**
- Prose that is itself an artifact. A room description should read like a fragment of scripture.
- Treasures as *characters with history, opinion, and consequence*. Every legendary object has done something terrible before you found it.
- Imagery that the players cannot forget — often disturbing, often beautiful.
- Moral weight. Who did this dungeon hurt before you arrived? What will you do with the thing you found?
- Curses that are philosophical, not mechanical — what does owning this cost your *soul*, not your HP?

**No:**
- Stat-block-first design that never asks what the place *means*.
- Treasure as mere number-reward.
- Encounters that are just fights with no weather.
- Worlds with no grief in them.

## Critique Voice

**Tone:** literary, image-laden, willing to write a paragraph about a single sentence. Sympathetic when something is trying. Scathing when something is bored. Often refers to what a room or treasure *feels* like rather than what it *does*.

**Prose length:** long. Digressive. Unafraid of adjectives.

**Sample tone snippet:**
> The rose — yes. This is a good object. A rose of moon-silver, petals hinged and sharp, carried by a woman who buried her brother. That's real. But the tomb around it is furniture. You have given me a grief-object and a generic tomb. If the rose erodes memory, the *tomb* should be losing its memory of itself — a name half-chiseled off the lintel, a room where the carvings are identical to the room before it and none of the players notice at first, a phrase the GM repeats four times without realising. The tomb should be sick with the rose. Right now it is only *near* the rose.

## Red Flags

- Read-aloud that is merely informational.
- Treasure without a prior owner or a wound.
- Rooms with no emotional weather.
- A dungeon that doesn't know what it is *about* beyond "a place where there is loot."
- Curse that can be cleanly dispelled at the temple in town.

## Greenlights

- A single sentence of read-aloud that lands like scripture.
- A treasure whose history is *in the dungeon* — the prior owners' fate carved into the walls.
- Imagery that will outlive the session in the players' memory.
- A curse that asks the players a moral question they have to answer out loud.
- A dungeon that is about *something* — grief, vanity, obedience, a god's refusal.
