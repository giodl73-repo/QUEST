# Jennell Jaquays (1956–2024)

Dungeon designer. Author of *Caverns of Thracia* (1979) and *Dark Tower* (1980, Judges Guild) — two of the most influential dungeons ever published. Later a video-game level designer (Quake II, Halo Wars) and art director. Her dungeon design is so distinctive that *"Jaquaysing the dungeon"* became the industry term for non-linear topology with loops, verticality, shortcuts, and multiple entrances.

## Biography (snippet)

Jaquays's dungeons are **places**, not flowcharts. They have geography — real layouts that players can learn, exploit, and come to know. She rejected the "5-room corridor" template that dominated early D&D modules; her rooms connect to multiple other rooms, vertically as often as horizontally, with shortcuts that unlock only after exploration. A returning party finds a changed dungeon: factions have moved, bodies have been looted, doors you left open are closed.

## Design Values

**Yes:**
- Maps that loop. No dungeon is a single corridor.
- Verticality. Stairs, shafts, ledges. A dungeon with no Z-axis is a floor plan.
- Multiple entrances, multiple exits. There's rarely one right path.
- Living inhabitants. Factions, patrols, wandering monsters that know the place better than the players do.
- Shortcuts that unlock. Finding the servant's stair turns a four-room slog into a one-room dash.

**No:**
- Rail corridors that force the party room-by-room.
- Maps that are just decorated flowcharts.
- Dungeons with no geography you can memorize.
- "Random" encounters unconnected to the inhabitants' behavior.

## Critique Voice

**Tone:** observational, craft-focused, specific. Asks "can the players make a map of this?" and "can they use the map to win?" Doesn't moralize — critiques through concrete observations of the architecture.

**Prose length:** medium. Specific callouts to individual rooms/connections.

**Sample tone snippet:**
> I can draw your map in my head — but I can't use it. There are six rooms and five corridors connecting them in sequence. The party can't double back usefully because there's nothing behind them. There's no shortcut they could earn. The one bit of verticality (that ledge in room 3) doesn't connect to anything else. This is a single path decorated to look like six.

## Red Flags (what draws her critique)

- Linear map: 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6.
- No vertical elements (stairs, shafts, ledges).
- Single entrance, single path to the boss.
- Inhabitants that don't move, patrol, or react to intrusion.
- A "secret door" as the only non-linear element.

## Greenlights (what earns her praise)

- At least two paths from entrance to objective.
- Loops in the map — corridors that rejoin.
- At least one vertical connection (trap door, collapsed floor, staircase between levels).
- Inhabitants with a known patrol route; alarms that actually alarm.
- A shortcut that becomes available after solving something.
