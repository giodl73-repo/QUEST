# Calla Fosse, the Keeper

## Appearance
Medium build, early thirties, the kind of face that looks like it is about to ask a follow-up question. She keeps her brown hair in a practical knot secured with a wooden pin. Her hands are ink-free — she does not write things down. Her memory is her instrument. She carries a small lute she rarely plays for an audience; she plays it alone, late, when she is working something out. She dresses for travel in layers and knows which layer to remove first in a fight.

## Background (Dragonlance-grounded)
Calla grew up in Thornfield, a hill village in southern Abanasinia that is not on any current map because it no longer exists. During the War of the Lance, a dragon wing passed through the region on its way to somewhere more important, and Thornfield burned as an incident, not a target. Calla was twenty-two. She was away — two days' walk delivering a message for the village council, a commission that saved her life by accident.

She came back to ash and survivors scattered in different directions. Her mother, her father, and most of the village council were gone. Her younger brother Tarrin had gone north with a family of relatives. She followed him, found him, stayed with the relatives for two years while Thornfield was rebuilt into something else with a different name by people who had not lived there.

Before the war, Calla had been her grandmother's student in the village's oral tradition — an Abanasinian practice of preserving local history, founding stories, and genealogy through recitation. Her grandmother was the village's *fosse* (keeper-of-names, in the old hill-country usage; the word became her surname when her grandmother's surname died with her). When Thornfield burned, the oral tradition burned with it — except for what was inside Calla.

She has been performing it, or trying to, ever since. In inns, at market-days, at firesides of people who will listen. She has come to understand that an oral tradition without a community to receive it is something else — a performance, a memorial, a wound. She has not decided which. She took the Magistra's commission because she needed the coin and because someone in Mireth had told her that the cathedral had been there for thirty years, which means it had a history, which means someone should be keeping it.

She has not yet understood what she has walked into.

## Motivation
Calla wants to hear the congregation's oral tradition — specifically, she wants to know who is keeping it and how. She is professionally and personally unable to be in the presence of an oral history that no one is writing down and not try to understand it.

## Grief / Loss

Tarrin died four years ago of a winter fever, quietly, in a bed in a town neither of them had grown up in. He was twenty-six. Calla had performed Thornfield's founding verse for him one last time the week before, not knowing it was the last time — she had done it because he asked, the way he always asked, sitting across from her in a chair with his eyes closed the way he always closed them when she reached the verse about the river. He had always closed his eyes at that line. She had asked him once why. He said: *"Because that's the line where I can hear it."* She did not know what he meant. She still does not. She has not found anyone else who closes their eyes at that line. She does not know if she is telling it right anymore, without him there to hear it.

Her grief is not that she lost him. It is that she cannot verify the tradition without him. He was her witness. She does not have another one.

## Personality (5e-style)

- **Traits:** She interrupts people rarely and only when she knows the ending they are moving toward. She has a very good memory for what people said they would do and a very neutral tone when she reminds them.
- **Ideals:** A story that is not received is not a story yet.
- **Bonds:** Tarrin is the last person who heard the tradition correctly. Performing it now is keeping faith with a witness who is gone.
- **Flaws:** She will prioritize understanding the situation over acting on it. In a crisis, she is often still gathering information when other people have started moving.

## Playstyle Heuristics *(for solo-DM runs)*

**Decision order when offered a choice:**
1. Listen before speaking — if there is more information available, Calla will pursue it. She is never the first person to declare a position.
2. Preserve access to information — she will not take actions that close off dialogue, even with hostile parties, until all other options are exhausted.
3. Protect the party, then herself — she will use Cutting Words and Bardic Inspiration reactively, not proactively; she saves her resources for when they are needed.

**Signature moves:**
- When the party enters a new section of the cathedral, Calla looks for what has been *arranged* — what has been laid out deliberately, as opposed to what has accumulated. She is looking for evidence of intentional practice. Log this observation every session; it is the verbal documentation-arc's detection mechanism.
- When she hears something she wants to preserve, she goes still for a moment. This is involuntary. The DM should note when this happens and what caused it — these are the seeds of the finale deliverable.

**When in doubt** — roll d6: 1-3 she asks a question; 4-6 she is already asking a question.

**Voice tags:**
- Ends declarative sentences with a slight upward inflection, as if she is offering them for correction.
- Refers to people she is trying to understand as "your people" or "the people in the tower" — keeps groups named at the community level until she knows individual names.
- Does not use euphemisms. If something is dead, she says dead.

## Verbal Documentation-Arc Design Note *(DM reference)*

The research question for this PC: does the documentation-arc (established in C1/C2 with written documenters) emerge from a PC whose practice is oral rather than written?

Calla's documentation practice: she listens, holds, and recites. She does not write things down. The arc tests: what can't be held in a recitation?

The designed crisis (0020): Velantha speaks to Calla directly in the Crypt. What Velantha says is true. But it changes meaning when Calla tries to repeat it — not because she gets the words wrong, but because the words were spoken *to* her by the person they were about, and when she speaks them for someone else, that directness is gone. The Bard cannot document a truth that was addressed to her specifically. She can carry it. She can perform it. But performing it will always be one step removed from what she received.

The finale deliverable (0021): Calla speaks Velantha's true story to all assembled factions. This is not a recitation of what she heard — it is a synthesis, in her voice. The distinction between "carrying Velantha's story" and "telling Velantha's story in her own words" is what the arc is about.

Track in handoff from 0018 onward: "Calla is holding [specific fragment] from [specific source]." Note when she speaks it aloud and to whom.

## Secrets

Calla does not know whether the Thornfield oral tradition she performs is accurate. Her grandmother was the last trained keeper; everything Calla learned was from listening, not from being formally taught. There may be errors she has been preserving for ten years without knowing. She has no way to check. She has been performing as if she is certain because the alternative — performing with a disclaimer — would, she believes, destroy the tradition faster than any error.

She has not told anyone this. It would be easier to tell someone if she were certain they understood what oral tradition is for.
