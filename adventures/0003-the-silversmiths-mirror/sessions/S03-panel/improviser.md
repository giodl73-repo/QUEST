## Critique

### Overall assessment

The DM said yes to the party's restraint, and the module was strong enough to absorb it. That is the test: when four PCs refuse the module's biggest-designed beat, does the session collapse or pivot? It pivoted. The destruction-rite became the session's climax; Kessa's Portent became the rite's rescue; Mira's ink-writing became the resolution. None of those were the *planned* climax. They were the *improvised* climax that the planned architecture enabled. That is design-that-yields-to-play at its cleanest.

### What worked

- **The DM let all four doubt-dice land and did not rescue the Option C beat.** The module had sunk design-effort into the Mirror-attunement scene — subagent prompt, Blank-Mirror read-aloud (P0 revision), campaign-continuity hook. All four PCs' doubt-dice rolled against looking; the DM could have nudged (Mira could have pleaded; one PC could have been pressured; the module had the hooks). Nothing nudged. The restraint stood. That is "yes" to the players' truth.
- **Kessa's Portent rescue of Grom's rite was an improvised PC-to-PC move.** The module did not specify that a wizard could Portent-replace a priest's Religion check on a forge-rite. The rules allow it (Portent replaces *any* d20 roll); the fiction doesn't specify forbid; the DM said yes. Kessa in-character: *"Vethrenn. I have never met you. If this was part of what you wanted me to see — let it be through me."* The DM framed her spent die as a benediction. The rite succeeded. That is RAW-compliant improvised narrative.
- **Mira's unscripted ink-writing (Scene 7).** Module did not script it. DM introduced it as Mira's response to the rite. This is the DM improvising an NPC completion-beat that the arc had earned. Mira asked Thera for a corner of her notebook; Thera complied; Mira wrote Seralen's name. Small gesture, large consequence.
- **Thera's non-lethal shortsword reversal on Pevra (Scene 3).** Not in the rules as a declared Fast Hands action; close to it. Thera's Scene 3 sneak attack dealt 16 damage vs Pevra's 11 HP — lethal math. The DM allowed Thera's "reverses her grip at the end of the swing" as a non-lethal choice at no mechanical cost. That is yes-rule improvisation. The alternative (force 5e's rule that you declare non-lethal *before* the roll) would have killed Pevra and lost the scene's intel-payoff.

### What needs work

- **Module said Kann should flee; dice said Kann dies.** The module's GM notes said *"Kann's fleeing is a feature. The campaign-structure benefits from Kann escaping."* Round 2, Kann at 5 HP, declared Dash south. Kessa's Magic Missile auto-hits — 8 damage — Kann drops at -3 in the street. The DM could have let Kann survive at 1 HP (narrative armor), or had him take cover mid-flight (Dodge action), or had Kessa's sheet-3rd-priority "Don't violate the Test" apply to fleeing targets. None of that. Kessa's heuristic said command-node, and the DM let dice resolve. The improviser respects this — the DM said yes to the dice, not yes to the module — but the campaign-continuity cost is real (no returning Kann for #0006/#0007). A softer improv would have let the module's intent survive. The harder improv let the dice win. Both are legitimate; I prefer the harder one, but flag it.
- **The filigree-dim could have been improvised to land.** Module says the dim happens upstairs when the cloth lifts; nobody was upstairs. An improvising DM could have had Harel — who was with Mira in the hearth — look up at that moment because a bird cried outside, and notice. Or Mira herself could have glanced out of habit. The DM held tight to positional rules (passive-Perception by the book). A less tight DM would have found the angle. Mildly.

### The question I'd push on

**When should "yes, and" override module intent?** This session had three Option-C-style moments: the Mirror decision (didn't fire), Kann's escape (didn't fire — Kessa killed him), and Grom's rite (fired with Kessa's Portent). One of three. The module's design-intents survived one of the three stress-tests. That is not a complaint — character agency (10/10) is the top-scored dimension — but it is a question worth answering before S04: *are there module-design intents that the improviser should preserve even when the dice say otherwise?* Campaign-continuity suggests yes (Kann's survival). Character agency suggests no. The rubric splits them at 10 and 10. Someone needs to call it.
