# Gary Gygax on *The Silversmith's Mirror*

## Overall verdict

This is a curious beast — well-keyed, tactically competent, with real resource pressure in Scene 5 and a map that rewards scouting. But it is not a dungeon. It is a moral decision wrapped in combat wrapper, and the moral heft is hauled by a one-long-sentence speech in Scene 4 that will not survive contact with a table that has grown distracted by the crackling fire. The Mirror itself is a strong artifact with mechanical teeth — the Blank-Mirror Trap is a proper trap, not a "gotcha." What fails is that the adventure *outside* the Mirror's mechanics does not bleed. Scene 5 is medium-hard but defensible. Scene 6 is a choice, not a challenge. This would be a 7 in my hands twenty years ago when I still believed in narrative weight; today it is a 6, held up by the cleanliness of the table-ready format.

## What works

1. **Scene 5's three-entry-point design.** Front door (Vend), back service door (Pevra), upstairs window via roof (Lorn). Attackers breach simultaneously; Kann covers from the street. The prep-tier branching (Tier A: party has surprise; Tier B: Kann advances, rushed defense; Tier C: Kann has surprise) is *good*. Scene 1 and Scene 3 choices have *felt consequences* in Scene 5's tempo.

2. **Kann's Commercial Disengage.** At HP ≤ 8, he makes a shortsword attack at advantage and then Disengages at regular movement. This is not a cowardly rule; it is a *professional* rule. Kann is not Keloth. He will not die for an artifact. He will flee. The remnants will collapse. This rhythm is Gygaxian — the dungeon-master (in Kann's case, the enemy leader) is competent until he is not, then he is *gone*. You do not get a final boss speech.

3. **The Mirror's attunement is fast.** Combat-pace, one look, attunement complete. Not a short rest. This means Scene 6's moral choice does not drown in mechanics. The decision is *Should I look?* not *How long must I meditate?* Proper.

4. **Mira's one long sentence in Scene 4.** It is overlong; yes. But it is *load-bearing*. Thirty years of refusal, spoken aloud once, in one exhalation. The sentence is the adventure's spine. If your table lets you read it slowly and does not interrupt, it lands. If they do interrupt, the DM can narrate onward. This is a *player-skill test*, not a *module flaw*, but it is a real risk.

5. **The cross-adventure recovery path.** Mira's sales ledger carries the S02 missed phrase: *"I will not look at the rose again."* Investigation DC 13 OR passive Perception 15. The DM has three fallback routes (party investigates directly, Mira offers the ledger, DM points to the entry). This is *proper rubric v1.2 design*. The signal does not disappear; it is gated at real DCs with passive fallbacks. Kessa gets weight.

6. **Kann's Stonfold finger-bone is a quiet plant.** He carries a non-Vaenshold-vein moon-silver artifact (Stonfold-made, Stonfold-family seed for #0006 and #0007). It does not foreground. A PC must search the body; Kessa must recognize the alloy shift (Arcana DC 10). If missed, it is a seed not planted. If caught, it is a *proper artifact-as-lore-signal* — the moon-silver vein is not monolithic; two families, two smiths, two approaches.

7. **Table-readiness is, in fact, good.** Every stat block is inlined. Read-aloud is preserved. DCs are all present. The wandering-pressure table is provided. Rubric v1.2 anchor-level symptoms have passive fallbacks. A DM who reads the module once can run it. There are no "see file X" stubs in the rooms. This is clean work.

8. **The Blank-Mirror Trap is a proper mechanical curse.** Permanent attunement; 30-day love-capacity loss; no restoration (Greater Restoration does NOT work). A PC who looks and sees no one has *narrated their own isolation*. The mechanics serve the story without drowning it.

## What fails

1. **This is not a dungeon; it is a town-encounter with a combat scene bolted on.** Seven keyed rooms across 24 hours. No verticality save Harel's window. No loops; no multiple paths to a goal; no factions shifting in response to party action. Scene 1 is the market square (flavor). Scene 2 is a shop-counter interview. Scene 3 is an optional alley encounter. Scene 4 is a fireplace conversation. Scene 5 is the assault (real combat). Scene 6 is a moral choice. Scene 7 is the aftermath. *This is a story sequence, not a dungeon.* Gygax's weights favor dungeon integrity (1.3×). The module scores a 6 here. It is not salvageable — the thing *is* a town-encounter — but it *hurts* the weighted total.

2. **Player agency is gated by DM-controlled NPC choices, not player choice.** Mira will not let the party into the lower chamber in Scene 2. Mira will not look into the Mirror herself (she says *nyet*) unless the party rolls Persuasion DC 20 — and even then she does not attune, she just weeps. The party's agency is constrained by Mira's agency. This is *narratively appropriate* (she is a person, not a door) but *mechanically limiting*. The party cannot shortcut Scene 4's disclosure; they cannot force entry to the chamber; they cannot *surprise-attack Kann* in Scene 3 without Scene 1's intelligence work. Every move is gated. This is not *bad* design — it is *tight* design — but it is not *player-agency* design.

3. **Scene 5's combat is well-balanced but will not surprise anyone at this table.** Four to five humanoid attackers, crossbows and scimitars, no special abilities or terrain. Kann is the only opponent with a named reaction (Commercial Disengage). Mira's involvement is narrative (she takes one swing if Harel is threatened) not tactical. The encounter is *competent* — the three entry points are *good*, and the prep-tier branching is *excellent* — but it is not *distinctive*. You could run this encounter with Kalashtar raiders, Nerakan thugs, or Solamnic deserters; the tactics would not change. The *feel* would change (Kann's lament for his wife, Pevra's grief for Idra, Vend's silence) — but the *combat math* is generic.

4. **Aelwen's Mirror does not reward treasure-seeking in the traditional sense.** The Mirror is not *loot you carry out and sell*; it is *a decision you carry with you for the campaign*. This is sophisticated; it is not *treasure-as-XP* in Gygax's sense. A PC who attunes the Mirror does not gain a magical advantage without *cost* — their self-image is permanently replaced. An unattuned Mirror is *incalculable loot* (the module says "priceless" for Aelwen's journal page; the Mirror itself is worth... what? 30 gp raw moon-silver? 300 gp as a magical artifact? No valuation is given). This *vagueness* is intentional — it forces moral deliberation — but it violates the Gygaxian principle that *treasure is experience, and experience is measured*. You cannot spend a Mirror. You can only *bear* one.

5. **Scene 4's disclosure scene hinges on DM execution, not module design.** Mira's one-long-sentence is ~100 words. It is a speech, not a moment. A table that has been rolling initiative in Scene 3 will not sit still for 100 words of NPC exposition. The module *knows* this — it says "read slowly" — but reading-slowly is not a guarantee. If Mira loses the room, Seralen's death (Scene 4's emotional core) becomes a *plot fact*, not a *felt weight*. The module *depends* on the DM's performance here. This is not a flaw in isolation — many modules depend on DM performance — but it is a flaw in a module claiming table-readiness. A truly table-ready module would break Mira's sentence into **three separate speeches**, each triggered by a question, each one or two sentences. The current module forces a DM choice: *go off-script to add brevity, or follow the script and risk losing the table.*

6. **The lower chamber (Scene 6) is a moral choice, not a mechanical challenge.** The party has won Scene 5; they have Mira's trust; they open the hidden door. The Mirror sits on a stand. They decide: *look or not? destroy or take?* No roll is required (attunement is voluntary). No skill check opens the chamber (Mira does it). The "scene" is five minutes of discussion. This is *appropriate* for a campaign-weighted decision, but it is not *table-ready in the Gygaxian sense*. Gygax would want a locked door (Investigation DC 13 to open without Mira), a guardian (Mira's hammer-swing if they try to force the chamber), or a trap (silver-filigree latch is described, but there is no *mechanical* trap if they trigger it wrong). The module *avoids* these because Mira is not an antagonist. But the result is that Scene 6 has no *failure state*. The party cannot *lose* the Mirror in the lower chamber. They can only *choose* what to do with it.

7. **Mira's exit (Scene 7) is written but not earned.** Mira has decided, pre-adventure, to leave for Stonfold. The assault does not change her mind; it accelerates her timeline. She says: *"I will leave Crystalmir today for Stonfold. Harel will come with me."* This is *narratively clean* — she has been waiting 30 years to give the Mirror away; the assault is her release — but it is not *game-mechanical*. The party's choices in Scenes 5 and 6 do not *determine* whether Mira leaves. They only determine *when*. If the party saves Mira but refuses to take the Mirror, Mira still leaves. If the party takes the Mirror and destroys it, Mira still leaves. If Harel dies, Mira does not leave *immediately* — she retreats into grief — but the module notes this as *"the adventure's worst outcome by Mira's lights,"* not *"the adventure's worst outcome by the party's."* Mira's arc is *sealed*, not *open*.

8. **The Blank-Mirror Trap is elegantly designed but narrowly triggered.** A PC attunes and sees no one = 30-day love-capacity loss. The trap *works* only if the DM knows each PC's grief-paragraph (dead lover, distant mentor, unmet ancestor). At a table where the party has shared character sheets: excellent. At a table where the DM has not read the sheets: the trap misfires. The module *requires* `personas/parties/varduin-muster/<pc-slug>.md` to be read before play. This is stated in the Option C subagent prompt — but the DM may not realize this until a PC reaches Scene 6. By then, it is too late to read the sheets. The module is *designed* for this; it includes the subagent-prompt fallback. But it is a *dependency risk*.

## Specific recommendations

1. **Scene 4: Break Mira's long sentence into three shorter speeches.** Currently: one ~100-word exhalation. Recommend: three speeches, each 2-3 sentences, triggered by three different questions: (A) *"What is in the cellar?"* → Mira names the Mirror. (B) *"Why can't you look?"* → Mira names Seralen and the 30-year refusal. (C) *"Why are the Bone-Collectors coming?"* → Mira names the Istaran client. Each speech is a *beat*, not a *paragraph*. The emotional weight is *preserved* (Seralen's name, the refusal, the client's hunger), but the pacing is *saved*. A table that would skip the long sentence will not skip three short answers to direct questions.

2. **Scene 5: Add a *trap-sense* check to Kann's position.** Currently, Kann is at the street covering with his crossbow; the party can hear him before Round 2. Recommend: if the party's Perception exceeds 13 (passive or active), they hear Kann's breathing at the front door *before* the assault begins (end of Scene 4). They get 1d4 rounds of prep. This is not a *trap* in the mechanical sense, but it is a *warning sign* that a careful party can spot. Gygax values this.

3. **Scene 6: Add a *choice point* at the chamber door.** Before Mira opens the hidden door, let the party make a *collective decision*: *"Are we ready to see it?"* This is not a roll; it is a **moment to step back**. If a PC says "No, I'm not looking into it," *let that choice be binding for that PC*. The Mirror sits on the stand. One PC looks; another does not. This preserves agency — a PC can opt out of attunement *before* the chamber opens.

4. **Scene 3: Formalize the "captured scout gives intel" sequence.** Currently: *"Intimidation DC 12 or Persuasion DC 14."* Recommend: add a **brief narration** of what the captured scout *remembers* (e.g., Pevra seeing Idra's face before she died; Lorn reciting the patrol-route; Gorr confessing he was afraid). This turns the *intel-check* into a *brief roleplay scene*. The facts (5 attackers, assault at midnight, Istaran client, Stonfold moon-silver) are the same, but the *delivery* is human.

5. **Scene 7: Offer Mira a *parting gift* option.** Currently, Mira leaves; the party does not choose her fate. Recommend: let Mira *gift one PC* with something from her shop — a silver cup with the Vaenshold rose, or one of Seralen's jewelry pieces. This is not mechanical (0 gp value); it is *narrative weight*. A party that spent three scenes building Mira's trust gets a *token* of that trust. She says: *"You kept Harel from harm. Take this. It is Seralen's. It was meant for someone who would understand."*

6. **Option C subagent prompt: Add a mandatory read-step.** Before a PC looks into the Mirror, the module must *require* the DM to have read that PC's sheet in `personas/parties/varduin-muster/<pc-slug>.md`. Add a note: *"STOP. Before this PC looks, read their full sheet (biography and voice; Decision Order and grief-paragraph). You will need this to adjudicate the reflection."*

7. **Rubric v1.2 anchor-level symptoms: Make the filigree-dim *louder* in Scene 5.** Currently subtle. Recommend: if a PC is upstairs defending Harel's room, they see the filigree flicker *three times* — once when the hidden door opens, once when the cloth lifts from the Mirror, once when the Mirror is lifted. Each flicker is a *Perception DC 12 active*. A PC who catches all three knows: *the Mirror just moved*.

8. **Kann's moon-silver finger-bone: Add a *description* when the party loots it.** Currently: *"30 gp raw; carries Kann's dead wife's last minute. Unattuned."* Recommend: *"A small finger-bone, moon-silver, worn smooth by 30 years of being carried next to Kann's heart. An engraving on the cord: Nerakan script, tiny. If deciphered: 'Felor.' The bone is cold. It has never been attuned. Kann has listened to his wife's last moment. He chose not to hear it."* This makes the bone *feel like an artifact*, not like *loot*.

## Final line

*The Silversmith's Mirror is a competent adventure with real table-readiness and a moral spine, but it is not a dungeon, and Gygax's D&D is made of dungeons.*
