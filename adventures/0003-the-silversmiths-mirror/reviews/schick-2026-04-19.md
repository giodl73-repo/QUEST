# Lawrence Schick on *The Silversmith's Mirror*

## Overall verdict

This is a module that knows exactly what it is and executes with craft. Aelwen's Mirror is a treasure with a *voice* — not just a mechanical artifact, but an inheritance of grief, refusal, and love frozen in silver and obsidian. The problem is that the module's architecture doesn't quite trust that premise enough. A signature treasure deserves a signature room, and what you've built instead is a lived-in town with good people and a combat encounter bolted on like scaffolding. The Mirror's genius (the blank-mirror trap, the self-image replacement, the 30-year refusal) is the entire reason to play this adventure. Everything else should serve that, and much of it does — but not all of it.

The night assault is competent and scalable. Mira is a real person. Kessa's lore-thread is earned. But Scene 5 (combat) could be anywhere, and that's a miss when your treasure is as singular as this one. A Schick-level adventure would put the Mirror's curse into the *dungeon itself* — make the rooms hum with the weight of what Aelwen made. As written, the Mirror is in a generic cellar, and the party's only path to it is through a standard combat encounter with standard scouts. That's not what you built. You built something stranger.

## What works

1. **Mira herself.** A 62-year-old woman who has trained herself not to want something for 30 years is a protagonist, not an NPC. The scene where she speaks her long sentence is the module's moral center, and it lands because you let her be real. Her refusal to look at the mirror is not a plot gate; it's a character's life.

2. **The blank-mirror trap.** This is what you should have led with. A treasure whose curse is *not knowing whether you can bear the truth about yourself* — whether anyone has loved you most — is the kind of high-concept conceit that makes me want to write a room around it.

3. **Aelwen's six artifacts as a world-building engine.** The Silver Rose, the Reliquary, the Mirror, and three unnamed others. This is *Legacy*. Heva rescuing one page from the pyre. Mira holding the Mirror across six generations without opening it. That's a campaign.

4. **The cross-adventure phrase recovery.** The ledger entry from S02 ("I will not look at the rose again") as a passive-Perception fallback in Scene 4 — that's craft. You built the failure-path into the module.

5. **Option C.** Spawning the PC as a subagent when they look at the Mirror is the right move. Let them describe their own reckoning with being-loved. That's table-magic.

6. **Mira's departure at Scene 7.** She closes the shop and leaves for Stonfold. Not saved, not killed, not rescued. *Departed.* That's an ending that echoes backward through the whole adventure.

## What fails

1. **The night assault is a filler encounter, not a treasure-defense.** Kann wants the Mirror, but the assault doesn't *feel like* a conflict over this specific artifact. The combatants are scattered scouts with standard tactics. In *White Plume Mountain*, the tentacled guardians in Blackrazor's room *exist because Keraptis put them there to test the sword's future wielder.* Your assault feels like it's blocking the way to the Mirror, not protecting it. The rooms should echo the treasure's gravity.

2. **Crystalmir has no verticality.** Market → shop-street → shop-interior → cellar. It's a line. You've built a great town, but you've made the exploration spatial-flat. A Jaquays-appropriate version might have the lower chamber accessible via multiple paths — the cellar *and* the town militia's armory, or via the innkeeper who once worked silver.

3. **The mirror's room is generic.** Six feet square, dry, oak stand, cloth-covered. This is where the *center of gravity* lives — where a PC might attune and have their self-image rewritten. That chamber should make the bearer *feel* Aelwen's presence. Where's the obsidian dust on the floor? Where's the evidence that this silversmith *grieved* in this room? What in the architecture tells me this is the Mirror's room and no other?

4. **Scene 3 (scouts in the alleys) is a soft gate.** The encounter is optional ("if the party notices Lorn"), and its only reward is prep for Scene 5. But if the whole adventure hinges on whether the party prepared, you need Scene 3 to be *mandatory and irresistible.* Harel tells them "they've been watching for six days." Make that active. A child pulls Kessa into the alleys. Mira sends word. Don't let this land as "if you think to investigate."

5. **The six artifacts are named and then abandoned.** Wrath-Coin, Pride-Circlet, Thread-That-Frays, and one unnamed. Aelwen made these in grief, each one a different sorrow. But you mention them in a single line ("Aelwen wrote, she made three more in secret...") and move on. These are seeds for #0004-#0007, which is good *planning*, but this adventure should *showcase* what Aelwen's vault means. What if the party found the Wrath-Coin in Mira's shop, forged centuries ago, and didn't realize it?

6. **The Bone-Collector threat feels external.** Kann is commercial and will bargain, which is good. But the Istaran client (Lady Tiran, Felor) is named only once and feels like a plant, not a presence. If Kann is the enemy, why? Make him *want* the Mirror for a reason that makes the party care — not just "a client bought the contract," but something Kann himself risks for.

## Specific recommendations

1. **Rewrite Scene 6 (the Lower Chamber) as a signature room.** This is non-negotiable. The chamber should be architecturally distinct. Obsidian dust. Candlestubs arranged in patterns (Aelwen's meditative ritual?). The stand itself should be engraved — not just oak, but oak with Aelwen's fingerprints, maybe a half-bloomed rose carved into the leg. When the PC enters, they should *know* this room was made to hold this specific treasure.

2. **Make Scene 3 unavoidable, not optional.** Have Harel pull a PC aside in Scene 2: "They watch the back door at night. Pevra and Lorn. Two of them. I have seen them." Now the party has intel, but they still choose how to use it. The gate opens, not the lock.

3. **Give the six artifacts air.** Even if you're not revealing the other four in this adventure, put one in the room. A pendant with a wrath-mark. Something Aelwen forged that Mira kept. Let the party see the *scope* of what this silversmith could make.

4. **Reframe the assault.** Kann doesn't want the Mirror because a client paid him. Kann wants the Mirror because *he once held a moon-silver artifact* (his Stonfold finger-bone). He knows what it costs. He knows what it's worth. Now he's trying to buy one more. That's a character arc, not a contract.

5. **Add a fallback path to the cellar.** If the assault goes sideways, the party should have discovered (or be able to discover) that Mira's kitchen has a drain to the cellar — something Aelwen built to bypass the main trapdoor. Let the party escape downward if they're losing the fight. That's agency, not railroading.

6. **Explicit Hint 4 in Mira's final scene.** The journal page names "an ingot I should have forged instead." Before Mira leaves for Stonfold, have her say to Kessa (if Kessa has the page): "The ingot she mentions — that is what you will look for at the end. That is what my line could not make." Don't leave it to assumption.

## Final line

The Mirror is a brilliant treasure living in a town-level adventure that doesn't quite know how to frame it; rewrite the room and Mira's decision becomes unforgettable.
