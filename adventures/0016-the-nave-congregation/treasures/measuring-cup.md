# Velantha's Measuring Cup

*A clay cup with a crack down one side sealed in tallow, sitting at the head of the nave as if it has always been there.*

## Appearance

Ordinary clay, fired to a dull red-brown. Wheel-thrown, not especially fine work — a household cup from a market stall, the kind sold in sets of four. The crack runs from the rim to just above the base, a clean line that would have made the cup useless for holding liquid except that someone, at some point, packed the crack with tallow and the cup has held ever since. The tallow is yellow with age but still seated. The rim is worn smooth on one side — the side Velantha held to patients' lips for twenty years.

The cup is always full of whatever it last held — or rather, it always appears to hold exactly the right amount. Set it down empty and it is empty. Pick it up intending to pour medicine and the amount in the cup is always correct. Not measured. Not calibrated. Simply right.

## Presence / Desire

The cup does not want to be decorative. It has been at the head of the nave for thirty years and it has been patient about this, but its patience is the patience of something that is waiting to be used. Pick it up with no purpose in mind and it feels ordinary — clay, old, slightly heavy. Pick it up intending to give something to someone who needs it and it settles in the hand with a rightness that is difficult to describe: not lighter, exactly, but more willing. The correct amount.

It is drawn toward need. It does not discriminate about whose need. The congregation has noticed that no keeper has ever spilled from it — not in thirty years of daily rites, not in the dark, not in a hurry. The cup spills when there is excess. The cup doesn't produce excess.

## Provenance

**1. An unnamed potter in Mireth-on-the-Hill (made ~50 years before campaign; sold at a weekly market).**
Six copper pieces. She made twelve that week; this one cracked in the kiln and she nearly discarded it. She sealed the crack with tallow and sold it anyway at a discount. She thought it would be used for dry goods. It wasn't.

**2. Sister Velantha (used ~20 years; died at the ritual site ~30 years ago).**
Velantha bought it for its price — she needed a vessel that could be cleaned, carried, and dropped without disaster. The crack meant it was never good for liquids that couldn't seep. She used it for dry medicines dissolved in water, or for tinctures, or for the small measured amounts that are the difference between helpful and harmful. She knew the correct dose for almost every patient after the first year. The cup learned alongside her. When she performed the ritual, the cup was in her satchel. She left the satchel at the gatehouse threshold. The cup was inside.

**3. Dova, First-Keeper of the congregation (kept ~5 years; died ~24 years before campaign).**
Dova found the cup in the satchel and understood immediately that it was a vessel for healing. She began using it for the congregation's daily water-sharing rite — a practice she invented without a template, based on what seemed right. Under her hands, the cup's properties emerged: the amount was always correct. She died never having spilled a drop in five years of daily rites. The congregation noted this. They began treating the cup as a relic before they had a word for what a relic was.

**4. Veth, current keeper of the nave (kept 22 years; still present).**
Veth has held the cup every morning for twenty-two years. He does not find its properties remarkable. He is simply careful with it, the way you are careful with a thing that has been in your hands every day for twenty-two years. When asked if the cup is magical, he says: *"It holds what needs to be held."*

## Properties (Mechanical)

### Rarity
Uncommon. **Attunement required** by a creature that has administered medicine, care, or comfort to another creature at least once (no restriction on class, race, or alignment — the condition is an act, not an identity).

### Benefits (while attuned)

- **Correct dose.** When the attuned creature uses the cup to administer a potion, tincture, herbal preparation, or any liquid intended to heal or restore, the cup always contains exactly the correct amount. The attuned creature cannot accidentally administer too much or too little from this cup. If administering a potion with a variable effect (e.g., Potion of Healing: 2d4+2), roll the dice — but the result is always treated as if rolled with advantage (roll twice, take higher). The cup calibrates; it does not create.
- **Steady hand.** The attuned creature cannot spill the cup's contents unintentionally. Forced movement, knockdowns, and similar effects that would normally spill a held vessel do not spill this cup. The creature must be rendered unconscious or the cup deliberately turned for it to empty.
- **Medicine mastery.** The attuned creature has proficiency with the Herbalism Kit if they lack it, or expertise (+double proficiency) if they already have it.

### Passive benefit (no attunement required)

Any creature that drinks from the cup — whether the attuned creature administers it or another — receives the benefit of the Correct Dose property for that single drink. The cup always holds the right amount for the person who needs it.

### Relic Test

The cup is not cursed. It has a test.

If the attuned creature uses the cup to measure or administer a harmful substance — poison, a damaging compound, anything intended to injure rather than heal — the calibration fails. The cup will never again hold the correct dose for anyone. It becomes an ordinary clay cup with a sealed crack, useful for dry goods or water, nothing more.

The DM adjudicates harmful use. The test is about intent. A healer who miscalculates a dose and inadvertently harms a patient does not fail the test. A creature who deliberately uses the cup to deliver poison fails it immediately.

The failure is permanent and silent: the cup does not break, does not change appearance, does not announce itself. The next time the attuned creature reaches for the correct amount, the amount is simply wrong.

### Ending the Relic Test consequence

The consequence is permanent for this cup. It cannot be restored. Another cup could be made that served the same purpose, but this specific cup — thirty years of daily rites, Dova's five years, Velantha's twenty — would be gone. The congregation would know. They would not be angry. They would simply be without the cup that was the center of their rite. That is its own consequence.

## Hooks

1. **The congregation will want it back.** Veth holds the cup at the head of the nave. The party can observe it, hold it briefly by Veth's invitation, or receive its property in the morning rite — but Veth will not give it up unless the party has understood what it is for, not just what it does. Theft would not just antagonize the Nave congregation; it would activate the congregation's memory of every time something was taken from them.

2. **The correct dose is evidence.** A wizard examining the cup's property will determine that the calibration comes from faith-generated magic — the same source as the field satchel's warmth. Both objects. Same source. The party has now encountered two objects with the same magical signature. This raises the question of what else Velantha left behind, and what it would mean for all of them to be in the same place.

3. **Maret and the cup.** The cup always holds the correct dose for whoever is holding it. Maret has been practicing a discipline for six years without knowing what it is *for*. When she holds the cup, the correct amount is the exact amount that her practice needed to be the practice it is. The DM should note this beat if Maret holds the cup. She does not need to understand it to feel it.

## GM Notes

The cup is a different kind of relic than the field satchel. The satchel is about warmth — the care that persists. The cup is about calibration — the knowledge of exactly what is needed. They are Velantha's two practices: the care that accompanies (the satchel, carried) and the precision that heals (the cup, used).

When the party first sees the cup at the head of the nave, it should be unremarkable. A cracked clay cup. This is intentional. Do not draw attention to it in the read-aloud unless the party specifically looks at the objects at the head of the nave. Let them find it.

When Veth holds it during the rite, the party may notice that the amount in the cup appears to be whatever the rite requires — no more, no less — without Veth measuring anything. This is the first signal. Do not narrate it. Let an attentive player notice.

The cup's properties activate upon being held with intent. "Just looking" produces nothing. "I want to understand what this does" produces nothing. "I'm going to help someone with this" produces the correct amount. The cup responds to purpose, not curiosity.
