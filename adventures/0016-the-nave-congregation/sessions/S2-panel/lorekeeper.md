## Critique

### Overall assessment

The highest Lorekeeper score of the campaign. The Atmospheric Landing dimension carries the weight here — the oral verse circulating through three separate utterances in one session, the child's drawing connecting gatehouse to nave, the thirty-year polish on the floor that Dren recognizes correctly. This adventure could only happen in a world where gully dwarves have been publicly decided to be incapable of faith.

### What worked

- **The oral verse's three utterances (Scene 2.2 Cold Pulses and Scene 2.2 rite):** *"She came to us in the dark, and she had what we needed, and she gave it."* The Lorekeeper approves of oral transmission that actually circulates — heard in R1, spoken alone in R3, performed in R2 rite, spoken again in R3, performed privately by Gorret at the cup. A living oral tradition behaves this way: it is not stored, it is spoken. The session showed the tradition living.

- **Veth's private doubt (Scene 2.7):** *"I don't know if I am doing it right."* This is the correct theological uncertainty for a thirty-year oral-transmission practitioner. In Dragonlance, faith without clerical confirmation is the post-Cataclysm condition — everyone is improvising, everyone is uncertain whether they are doing it correctly. Veth's uncertainty is era-appropriate without requiring the Lorekeeper to cite a sourcebook.

### What needs work

- **"Gully dwarves" unglosed in the session log again.** The S1 panel's convergent suggestion (add "Aghar — considered incapable of sustained faith" on first use) was not applied in S2's log narrative. Two sessions now. The Lorekeeper is docking module fidelity one point for the ongoing omission. **Fix for S3's log:** add the gloss at first gully dwarf mention in the session summary. One sentence.

- **Mishakal has appeared in two adventures (R5 of 0015, R4 of 0016) without a gloss.** The S1 panel flagged this. The S2 session log mentions the rite's water offering to the altar stone without connecting it to Mishakal's identity as healing goddess. The Apse is being used as a Mishakal sacred space — but a reader unfamiliar with the setting doesn't know that. Add one gloss by S3.

### The question I'd push on

**Is Veth's practice a Mishakal rite or a Velantha rite?** The congregation came to this site because Velantha died here. The rite they perform involves water, a cup, a verse, and an altar — which is also Mishakal's standard ritual form (healing goddess, water symbolism, communal cup). Did the congregation independently recreate a Mishakal rite from first principles? Or did Velantha teach them something that was already a Mishakal practice, which they have preserved without knowing its theological grounding? This question is Lorekeeper-level: it affects whether the cathedral's official consecration would accept the congregation's rite as valid under Mishakal's doctrine. Design an answer by the time the party reaches the Keystone.
