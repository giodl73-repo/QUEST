## Critique

### Overall assessment

The most legible session of Campaign 4. The situation is immediately comprehensible — someone sabotaged the arrows, here is the evidence trail, here is the person, here is what he was forced to do — and the stakes are translated into physical terms throughout. The investigation reads as a story, not a skill-check sequence. The combat is the same: two rounds, a grapple, a speech, the thing stops. A new player can follow every step. My concern this session is Caelith's Portent. The die was held for three sessions, and when it fires, the log says: *"Caelith replaces Wil's d20 roll (12) with his Portent die [7]."* A new player knows what "replaces" means but not what Portent is, where it comes from, or why Caelith has been carrying a number since before this session. The mechanic worked; the fiction did not fully translate it.

### What worked

- **Scene 3.2 — The investigation reads as physical evidence, not checks:** *"Talis reads the cache in ninety seconds. 'This isn't rot — it's applied. Middle-tier racks only, consistent with knowing the rotation. Someone who lives here did this fast and knew where to look.'"* A new player does not need to know what Survival DC 12 means to follow this. The fiction tells the story: deliberate rot, applied by someone with inside knowledge, systematically placed. The dice are in the log for audit purposes; the scene gives a new reader everything they need. Then: *"Wil Fen. West wall watch, seven years."* Simple. The three-check investigation produces a three-beat discovery sequence with no dead space. This is how investigation scenes should work.

- **Scene 3.3 — Combat ends through Davan's words:** *"'We found the letter. We know about Varduin. We know about your mother. We know you didn't want to do this.' Wil stops moving."* A new player does not need to know Wil's stat block morale conditions to understand what just happened. Davan said the one true thing, and the combat stopped. The translation from mechanics (Wil's dual stop-condition fired) to fiction (Wil stops moving) is seamless. No rules vocabulary required. The scene works as fiction first and as mechanics second. This is the session's strongest accessibility moment.

### What needs work

- **Caelith's Portent: three sessions of carried mechanics, one sentence of translation:** The session log says *"I see this coming"* — which is Caelith's in-fiction framing, and it is good. But a new reader arriving at this log doesn't know what Portent is. The previous session's handoff mentions *"Caelith Portent [6, 7] CARRIED"* — a running campaign fact that assumes familiarity with the Divination Wizard class feature. When the die fires in Scene 3.3, the log explains the mechanical substitution but does not explain what Caelith experiences. Does he dream the numbers? See them in the air? The fiction-layer framing is one sentence; the rule-audit trail is four sentences. For a new reader, the ratio is backwards. One additional line of narration — *"The number he has been carrying since dawn, the one he has not yet used — he places it over Wil's future"* — would make the mechanic legible without explaining the mechanics.

- **Supply Die drop to d6: mentioned in party state, not given a scene:** The Supply Die steps from d8 to d6 after the combat. The log notes this in the party state table but does not translate it into the fiction of the session. What does d6 mean at Thorngate? Fewer potions, thinner reserves, a garrison that is visibly more stretched. A new player looking at "Supply Die: d8 → d6 (combat fired)" does not know what changed. The fiction-forward equivalent — something Hessa says at the healer's station, something Maret mentions, some physical sign that the garrison's resources just got slightly worse — would close the gap. The campaign mechanic is well-designed; it needs one sentence of in-fiction grounding per session.

### The question I'd push on

*"I knew what I was doing and I told myself it didn't matter. That's the part I can't fix by telling him."* Hessa gives the token to Sava, not the party. A new player reading this knows Hessa trusts Sava specifically — but why? The log doesn't explain it beyond the annotation that *"she trusts Sava to carry things correctly."* For a returning reader of Campaign 4, this is sufficient: Sava has been carrying things correctly for three sessions, two garrison healers have recognized this, the pattern is established. For a new reader arriving at this session, the reason is not visible. One line — either Hessa's internal logic or a DM narration note — would make the specificity of the token-to-Sava moment accessible without removing the restraint that makes it work. The healer-to-healer trust chain is the session's best running thread; it should land for a new reader the first time, not require S2 context to read correctly.
