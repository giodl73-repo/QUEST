## Critique

### Overall assessment

Four SURPRISE tags from four different PCs across five scenes. That's the best per-PC distribution of surprise in Campaign 4 to date. The previous sessions had asymmetric surprise (S2: Sava twice, Caelith once, Davan zero). S3 corrects that: Caelith hands the letter to Davan (Caelith), Caelith announces Portent preparation (Caelith), combat ends through naming (Davan), Hessa gives to Sava (NPC). Three PCs touched a surprise moment. The structural improvement is real. My concern is the same concern I had in S2: the module delivered its designed beats cleanly, and clean delivery caps surprise. Wil was the only off-book variable in a session where everything else ran to type. Wil did exactly what his stat block said he would do. The surprise came from what the PCs did with the beats they found, not from anything the module didn't see coming.

### What worked

- **Scene 3.3 — Caelith announces Portent preparation:** *"I see this coming."* Three sessions of carrying dice without context, and now the mechanic has a voice. He says it quietly, to no one, holding the Portent die. This is a yes-and moment that the module explicitly designed (the Portent fire was pre-designed for this scene) and that exceeded its design by adding the in-fiction framing. The announcement makes the moment. Without it, the Portent substitution is a mechanical footnote. With it, Caelith is present in his own combat — a Divination Wizard who literally sees the future and chooses the moment to show it. That is a player doing something with their class feature that the module could not have scripted. The design created the structural opportunity; the voice created the scene.

- **Scene 3.3 → 3.5 — Davan names the coercion, then carries the letter:** Two separate improvisations from the same PC in the same scene cluster. First: he names the coercion rather than continuing to fight — choosing the statement over the damage. Second: he puts the letter in his breast pocket beside Tomek's token. The second beat is purely Davan — not prompted, not flagged, not a scene the module wrote. The letter and the token are the same category of thing to him. He carries them together. The module gave him the corridor; the PC gave it this specific content. That is an improviser's favorite kind of contribution: the module creates the space, the player fills it with something better than the module had planned.

### What needs work

- **Talis missed and that was it:** Scene 3.3 — Talis fires her shortbow (total 11 vs AC 13, miss). The miss is honest and logged correctly. But Talis's only contribution to the combat is a missed shot. From an Improviser's perspective, a PC who fires and misses in round 1 and does not have an initiative slot in round 2 (combat ends in two rounds) has zero improvisational presence in the session's most kinetic beat. The failure-as-tactical-reframe innovation from S2 (Talis repositioning after failed climbs) was the right kind of off-book contribution. S3 did not give Talis a moment to make her failure count for anything. The investigation phase did (Survival 21 is strong), but the combat was over before she could do anything with her position. Module design for S4: give Talis a scene where her instinct — not her dice — is what matters.

- **Wil as a purely reactive antagonist:** Wil follows his stat block exactly. He positions at the crossbow ledge. He shoots at the grappling PC. He tries to escape. He stops when his conditions fire. The Improviser appreciates correct stat-block behavior; this is not a complaint about the adjudication. The complaint is that Wil never surprised anyone. An NPC antagonist who does only what his stat block says adds nothing to the improvised layer of the session. The most interesting version of Wil would have been one moment where he chose something his stat block didn't require — a hesitation before shooting, a word when named, anything that suggested a person rather than a condition-triggered state machine. His arc-completion (coercion named → stops) is character logic correctly designed. His combat behavior was entirely mechanical. The Improviser wants one beat of NPC improvisation from each session's central antagonist.

### The question I'd push on

Caelith's notebook has the contractor message drop point. He does not share it with the party. The session log flags this as an open thread. The Improviser's question is not "why didn't he share it" — it is "what happens in S4 when the party needs this information and Caelith has to decide whether to produce it?" That is the most interesting improvised scene the session has set up. The drop point is not just intelligence; it is a question about what Caelith does with knowledge he accumulates privately. His character register (institutional-analysis, notes-book, reads information as belonging to the person best positioned to use it) means this will resolve through his sheet logic, not through a Persuasion check. The Improviser wants to see the scene where someone asks him what's in the notebook and he has to choose. Set that scene up before S4 ends.
