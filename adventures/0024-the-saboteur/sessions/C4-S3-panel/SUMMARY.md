## Strongest Consensus

**Character Agency (10/10)** drew the highest marks the panel has awarded in Campaign 4. All five lenses independently cited Davan’s naming-rather-than-damage as the session’s primary achievement. The Roleplayer noted the corridor beat as the session’s best character work; the Improviser cited it as a player filling module-created space with content better than the module planned; the Tactician acknowledged tactical position enabled the naming without diminishing it; the Fresh Face called it the session’s strongest accessibility moment; the Lorekeeper noted it as a Dragonlance-native characterization pattern earned without exposition. Five lenses, five independent citations of the same beat.

**Hessa’s arc-completion (token to Sava specifically)** was the second-most-cited moment, with four of five lenses noting it. The healer-to-healer trust chain (Maret S2, Hessa S3) produced a structural throughline the panel recognizes as a design achievement.

---

## Most Dissent

**Surprise (8/10)** produced the widest spread of concern from the same direction at different intensities.

- The **Improviser** gave credit for four surprise tags with three-PC distribution: best distribution of Campaign 4. Also flagged Wil as a reactive antagonist who never surprised anyone.
- The **Tactician** is not concerned about surprise count; the concern is that the encounter did not produce pressure. Zero party damage, unused garrison potion, encounter decided by the grapple.
- The **Roleplayer** and **Lorekeeper** both noted the session’s surprises were PC-authored (Caelith’s voice, Davan’s naming, Hessa’s token) rather than module-emergent.

The philosophical issue: C4-S3 is a disciplined session that executed its design without module-side surprises. The PCs improvised within clean rails. The gate’s Surprise 8 correctly reflects the session’s mode.

## Convergent Suggestions (multiple lenses agree)

1. **Talis needs a character-specific beat in S4** (Roleplayer + Improviser). S3 contribution is Ranger-class-shaped rather than Talis-Oremm-specific. By session 4, her sheet needs to surface.

2. **Caelith’s notebook and the contractor drop point** (Improviser + Lorekeeper). The unshared intelligence is a setup requiring a payoff scene. The Improviser wants the scene where someone asks what is in the notebook. The Lorekeeper wants to canonize the drop point before it becomes plot-relevant.

3. **Wil’s encounter design was under-pressured** (Tactician + Improviser). A single garrison soldier vs. four PCs with correct action economy is not a meaningful combat. S4’s encounter design should cost the party something tangible.

4. **Supply Die drop needs fiction-grounding per session** (Fresh Face + Lorekeeper). The d8 to d6 step is a mechanical notation without in-fiction translation. A garrison visibly more stretched after each combat should have one line of physical evidence per session.

## Questions Raised About the Module or the System

- Combat-end-through-naming (I-C4-S3-01) does not fit existing player-style definitions. The Tactician’s question — does it require dominant tactical position? — is the right design question. If naming requires tactical advantage, it is a conditional mechanic. If it fires from any tactical position, it is a new type of player agency worth tracking.
- Caelith’s voiced Portent (I-C4-S3-02) is the most interesting Mechanical Fairness innovation of Campaign 4. If Caelith in-voices his Portent deployment a second time in S4, the cross-character Portent compound path (v1.4 Portent-as-benediction) is re-openable.
