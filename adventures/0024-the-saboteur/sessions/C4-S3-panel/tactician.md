## Critique

### Overall assessment

A technically clean session. The combat math holds up under audit: Davan's initiative 21 grapple (Athletics 23) was the correct opening move, correctly rewarded by giving him the decisive advantage. Wil's two missed attacks (8 and 9 vs AC 18) are honest misses — at +4 to hit against AC 18, Wil hits on a 14, meaning he needed rolls of 10+ on the die and got 4 and 5. Sacred Flame's DC 13 correctly caught a +2 save. The Portent deployment is the session's mechanical highlight: Wil's escape attempt (Athletics +3 vs DC 14) succeeds on natural 12 (15 ≥ 14); the Portent [7] replacement produces 10, which fails the same DC. The substitution is correct by Divination School RAW. The Tactician's concern is not with any of this. The concern is that the combat resolved in two rounds with 0 party damage because Wil, a garrison soldier, was already outmatched on action economy (1 attacker vs 4). The module needed this combat to produce tension; it produced competence instead. Clean execution is not dramatic execution.

### What worked

- **Scene 3.3, Round 1 — Davan's initiative grapple:** Initiative 21, Athletics 23 vs Wil's contest roll (needs a 20 to beat; doesn't get it). The grapple fires before Wil can set his crossbow position. This is correct tactical adjudication: a high-initiative PC with a grapple specialist build should be able to foreclose a ranged opponent's range advantage in the first exchange. The module built the encounter with this as the intended opening — it paid off mechanically exactly as designed. No DM fiat required; the math delivered.

- **Scene 3.3, Portent deployment — audit trail:** The session log documents the original roll (natural 12, +3 = 15), the Portent replacement (7, +3 = 10), and the DC (14). Every number is on the table. The Portent [7] surviving three sessions to deploy here against Wil's escape roll rather than being squandered on a trivial check earlier is the session's mechanical wisdom. The Tactician notes: the timing was correct. Wil's escape on natural 15 would have ended the confrontation differently. The die was held for the right moment.

### What needs work

- **The combat threat level is too low for four sessions in:** Wil is a garrison soldier — +4 to attack, 18 HP, AC 13, single weapon (crossbow). Against a party of four, including a Fighter with AC 18 who goes first on initiative 21, the encounter was decided in the grapple. Sava's Sacred Flame dealt 6 radiant. Talis missed. Wil missed twice. Two rounds, 0 party damage. The Supply Die dropping to d6 is the session's mechanical consequence — not HP loss, not resource expenditure, not a real combat cost. At session 3 of 7, the encounter design should be producing pressure that costs the party something tangible. The garrison potion from the Supply Die was available; it was not needed. An encounter where the emergency resource is never approached is an encounter without stakes.

- **Caelith's Portent [6] is now one die and still undeployed:** The [7] fired correctly. The [6] is still carried. By session end, Caelith has been carrying at least one Portent die for four sessions without a long rest. The [6] is below AC 13 (Wil's AC) but above most ability check DCs the investigation offered. The Tactician wants to know: what is S4's encounter design planning to do with a Portent [6]? A die that low is useful for replacing enemy attack rolls or for forcing a low result on a contested check. If S4's encounter has no mechanic that can use a [6], Caelith's primary class feature remains inert for a fourth consecutive session. That is a mechanical fairness problem that compounds with each session.

### The question I'd push on

Davan ends combat by naming the coercion rather than continuing to fight. Wil is at 12/18 HP. The round 2 audit roll shows Wil would have missed anyway (total 9 vs AC 18). Does the naming mechanic require the party to be in a winning position? If Wil had rolled 18 on his round 2 attack — if he had hit Davan and knocked him back — would the naming have worked the same way? The combat ended through naming because Davan had him pinned at the parapet with backup in the room. The narrative agency is real; the tactical position made it possible. The Tactician's question: is "combat-end through naming" a player-style that fires generically, or does it require a dominant tactical position first? If the latter, it is a different kind of achievement than the gate's Character Agency 10 framing suggests.
