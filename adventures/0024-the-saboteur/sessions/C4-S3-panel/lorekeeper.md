## Critique

### Overall assessment

The session's most durable material is the contractor network. The seal device — appearing first in S2 on the fleeing scout's token and here on the coercion letter recovered from Wil's bunk — gives the campaign's antagonist network a physical signature that persists across adventures. This is exactly the kind of setting-texture that distinguishes the Thorngate Watch from generic garrison fiction: the same seal, same device, same network, working the keep from two directions simultaneously. The Lorekeeper's concern this session is the same as last session: the Reconstitution has been described by its operations for three sessions without acquiring doctrinal texture. We know what they do; we don't know what they believe about why they're doing it.

### What worked

- **Scene 3.2 — The contractor seal as cross-adventure evidence thread:** *"The seal's device on the surviving corner: the contractor's token device — the same one described in the S2 choke point context. Same network."* The log makes this connection explicit, correctly. A physical evidence trail crossing two adventures is a setting-weight detail: the contractor network is operational enough to run a coercion campaign inside the garrison while simultaneously conducting a supply-line interdiction outside it. This is not improvised faction behavior — this is a functioning organization with layered operations. The seal is the physical proof. The Lorekeeper notes: this detail should be logged in `reference/dragonlance/workshop-canon.md` with the device's specific properties (described as "contractor's token device" — what is it? A knot? A sigil? A pressed insignia?). Canonizing the device before S4 ensures the network's physical evidence trail remains consistent.

- **Scene 3.4 — Hessa's six months of garrison status updates:** *"She told herself it was harmless. The coin helped."* This is the cleanest characterization of how organizations compromise individuals: not through ideology, not through threat alone, but through incremental disclosure that was framed as harmless. Each piece of information passed is small; the accumulation is not. The Lorekeeper notes that this is a Dragonlance-native characterization pattern — the Cataclysm-era compromise of institutions through acceptable increments — and it does not need to be flagged as such in the session. It works without the glossing. The pattern is present in the fiction; the setting resonance is earned.

### What needs work

- **Varduin-by-the-Pines appears without a gloss:** *"the demand to disable the west wall cache, a two-day window. The seal's device on the surviving corner: the contractor's token device."* Wil's mother's address is in Varduin-by-the-Pines. This is not Varduin (the campaign's home base, named and established across Campaign 1). It is a different settlement — specifically *Varduin-by-the-Pines*, named to distinguish it. The session treats it as a known location. A new reader would not know whether Varduin-by-the-Pines is the same as Varduin or a different place. One word of clarification — in the module's NPC notes or the session's scene narration — would close the gap without breaking the scene's momentum. If it IS the same Varduin, that is significant campaign continuity (Davan knows this place); if it is a different settlement with the same root name, that should be established.

- **The Reconstitution: three sessions without doctrinal texture (third note):** This is the third consecutive session where the Lorekeeper has logged the same gap. The Reconstitution conducts layered operations — supply interdiction, garrison intelligence cultivation, coercion of personnel — with apparent organizational sophistication. None of that sophistication has been given doctrinal grounding. What do Reconstitution members believe about the Solamnic order they are opposing? What do they believe about the garrison itself? Pren (S2) had a principled relationship to the faction's operations; Wil (S3) was a coerced asset. Neither gave us the faction's interior life. The Lorekeeper will not raise this flag again after S3; by S4 it becomes a structural gap that the campaign cannot close retroactively without awkward exposition.

### The question I'd push on

Caelith has the contractor message drop point in his notebook. He has not shared it with the party. The Lorekeeper wants to know what the message drop point is — not as character-development interest but as setting-canonization priority. If the drop point has a specific location (a waymark, a dead tree, a loose stone near the postern gate), that location should be canonized before it becomes relevant to the plot. Drop points in siege-adjacent coercion networks have specific properties in Dragonlance-era military fiction: they are deniable, they have a counter-signal protocol, and they can be watched without the network knowing. If Caelith's notebook contains this information, it is the party's most significant intelligence advantage heading into S4 — and the fact that it is not shared with the party is either a character-specific choice (Caelith holds intelligence until it has a use) or a module gap (the session did not create the scene where sharing was the correct move). Which is it?
