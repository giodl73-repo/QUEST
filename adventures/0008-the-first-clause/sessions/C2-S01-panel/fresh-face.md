## Critique

### Overall assessment

This session is the most accessible in the workshop's history for a new player. No combat, no conditions, no spell-slot tracking — a new player can follow everything that happens by reading the dialogue. The log translates the shard's mechanical properties (dimming = detecting lies; warmth = recognition; glow = truth-finding) into immediately legible behaviors. The session's single biggest accessibility win is that the artifact explains itself through observable behavior in scene, not through exposition.

The Table Readiness score of 5 is earned: zero dice means there is nothing in this log to show a new player what their character sheet does mechanically. A new player who sat down for Session 1 would have a wonderful emotional experience and no idea whether their class abilities matter.

### What worked

- **The shard's properties are introduced through behavior, not through a stat block.** Sevel lies; the shard dims. Three times, with three different lies, so the pattern is clear by the third repetition. Henneth names the pattern for the new player: *"Is that why it dims when Sevel lies?"* This is excellent in-fiction glossing — the NPC asks the question the new player would have asked, and the answer is in the scene. No one has to explain "this item has a passive lie-detection property." The world shows it.

- **Tessamine's archive scene is an accessible entry point.** A new player arrives at Tessamine's archive, gets briefed on the objective (recover compact clauses), receives a concrete goal (Henneth's display case), and exits the scene with the shard in hand within 26 minutes. The arc is: briefing → complication (Sevel) → resolution (Henneth gives over the shard) → emotional capstone (Thessaly's wound). That is a complete, legible structure for a first session.

### What needs work

- **"White Robe," "Test," "Arcana" — these are introduced without glossing.** The log says Thessaly produces Tessamine's letter "before the guard is done formulating his question (decision-order: White Robe oath → knowledge first → witnesses)." A new player reading this log doesn't know what a White Robe is. The session doesn't gloss it. The Accessibility Policy requires first-use glossing of setting-specific terms; this session misses the opportunity for Thessaly's White Robe status to be quietly explained.

- **The "Test" in Thessaly's wound-naming is not explained.** She says: *"I cast a spell at my Test that I was not supposed to cast."* A new player has no context for what "the Test" means — this is a High Sorcery term of art (the Test of High Sorcery, the ritual that determines a mage's robe color and, potentially, costs them their life). Tessamine doesn't gloss it. Calder doesn't ask. The new player hears "Test" and may infer a magical trial, but the weight of the term — the fact that failing the Test kills you; that casting an unauthorized spell may have endangered a witness — is invisible.

### The question I'd push on

If a new player sat down for Session 2 and wanted to know who Thessaly is, the log would tell them: a White Robe who cast an unauthorized spell at her Test. But they wouldn't know what any of those three terms mean. The Fresh Face wants the next session to let at least one of these terms breathe in-fiction — a line from Orik or Calder that treats the Test as something the world knows about.
