## Critique

### Overall assessment

The Improviser's core question is "when I went off-book, did the DM say yes?" — and this session is unusual in that almost nothing was off-book. Every action the PCs took was anticipated by the module and rewarded cleanly. This is a double-edged result: it means the module was designed with genuine player-behavior foresight (high praise), but it also means there was no moment where the DM had to invent fairly on the fly. The Improviser is genuinely uncertain whether this is better or worse than a session with three off-book attempts and one good "yes."

The six documented surprises are all heuristic-emergent rather than off-book-emergent — Thessaly read the inscription before opening the case (anticipated), named the clause immediately (anticipated), named her wound (the arc-completion condition was exactly this). The DM never had to adjudicate a genuine improvisation because the players never tried anything the module didn't cover.

### What worked

- **Thessaly produced Tessamine's letter before the guard finished formulating his question.** The module listed the letter as an option; the player used it at exactly the right moment, in exactly the right sequence. The DM rewarded this with the guard recognizing the seal immediately and waving them through without a roll. This is a "yes-and" in structure even if it's not strictly off-book — the player's instinct to use a credential proactively was met with full friction removal. Consequence: the session moved to the study scene faster than any other route would have allowed.

- **Sevel's cargo-manifest discrepancy.** Calder noticed the numbers were off. The module didn't require anyone to notice this. Calder's decision-order (*"what is the weight of this?"*) fired and he registered it without acting on it. The DM correctly let this land as a loose thread rather than resolving it — Henneth also noticed. This is a yes-and in the purest form: the player added something to the fiction, and the DM confirmed it without resolving it. The Improviser respects a DM who knows when not to close a thread.

### What needs work

- **The session lacked a genuine off-book attempt with risk.** Every player action was either anticipated or was clearly the "right" action for the situation. The Improviser's most important criterion — "the DM said yes to at least three off-book attempts" — cannot be verified here because no off-book attempts occurred. A session where nothing surprises the module is a session the module designed too tightly.

- **What happens if the party had tried to steal the shard?** This is the test question the Improviser always asks. The log doesn't show any tension between the party and Henneth because the party behaved optimally. A real improviser at the table might have tried to pocket the shard and figure out the politics later. The module should have a graceful response to this. It probably does. But we can't tell from this log.

### The question I'd push on

The module delivered 100% manifest symptoms and Route D prerequisites in a single session. This is impressive design fidelity. But the Improviser wants to know: if the party had tried Route C (deception — getting Sevel to "confess" the cargo-manifest discrepancy to force Henneth's hand), what would have happened? The log doesn't show this path being tested. The Improviser wants the next session to include at least one moment where the players break from the obvious path.
