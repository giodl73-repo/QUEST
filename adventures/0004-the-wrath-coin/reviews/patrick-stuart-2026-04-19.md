# Patrick Stuart on *The Wrath-Coin*

## Overall verdict

This is strong work — architecture-grade. The Wrath-Coin lives; Aelwen lives in it; the curse is philosophical *and* mechanical, which is the harder thing. The adventure knows what it is about (grief-coils and what they cost) and does not flinch from it. The emotional payload is rare: Duren's burn-scar stopping, the apron-scrap diptych, Sigga's three years of weight. These are things that will stay with players.

But — and this is the reservation — the prose is sometimes thin where it should sing. Read-aloud in Scene 2 is working-prose, not Scripture. Krun's opening speech wants to be a meditation on fifty years of grievance and lands as bureaucratic despair. The module knows what it means to hold rage that long, but doesn't always *write* it that way. One more pass on voice.

## The Coin as object

Yes, it lives. *This* is what I meant when I wrote about treasures having history and opinion.

The Wrath-Coin does not want to be attuned. It wants to be emptied and destroyed — Aelwen's final word on it, written the night before she hanged herself. *"Do not let them hold what is not theirs."* The coin carries *six grievances* but is waiting for a seventh: the person who attunes it. Their anger becomes the coin's newest primary grievance, re-keying them against their own will. It's a trap that teaches philosophy.

Krun spent fifty years planning to use this thing to identify his father's killer. The killer died of plague two years ago. The coin knows; Krun does not. So when Krun finally attunes (or attempts to), the coin re-keys his rage against *Sigga's* rage at *him* for murdering her husband. His fifty years collapse in one round. *The rage-holder becomes the rage-held.* This is not a trick — it's an indictment.

Aelwen lives in it in two ways: the fully-polished mirror-face (she achieved her standard only once; everything else was unfinished) and the upside-down rose (inverted from the Mirror's rose opening and the Silver Rose's rose closing — Aelwen's artistic obsession made mechanical). The coin is Aelwen's redemption-work, which is why she intended it destroyed.

The Voidmouth is the *real* curse. Attuners who empty the coin and keep it believing they've won? The coin refills itself within 30 days, listening to anyone in a mile radius who feels wronged. It's brilliant because it's unknowable until it's too late. The adventure does not lecture about this; it plants it and trusts the DM.

One note: the mechanics are very clean, but Scene 6's description of Krun's half-second decision — that moment when he realizes he's been played by his own coin — needs more prose. One paragraph of Krun's internal collapse in real-time would make the whole sequence sing.

## What works

**Sigga.** She is not a quest-giver; she is a widow with information she took three years to decide to share. The phrase *"Here is the thing"* is character-perfect. Her last line in Scene 7 (*"I did not ask you to. I did not ask you not to. I will sit with the weight."*) is Scripture. If Grom plays honor-debt, Sigga is the moral center.

**The apron-scrap diptych.** Aelwen's fragment from #0003 (*"I should have forged an ingot"*) paired with the apron-scrap (*"This will be my good work. Empty it. Break it."*) is the kind of through-line that wins entire campaigns. Kessa holds both pieces and *feels* Aelwen's final thought-reversal. Storytelling through artifact archaeology.

**Reorx's Judgment (Hint 3).** *"A grief-coil can be uncoiled."* This lands silent (craft-witness reception) and lets Grom act on it before he articulates it. *This is trust.* And it unlocks Grom's Option C (Reorx-exile) which is the golden path if he has the wisdom to invoke it.

**The hearth-greeting saves Grom's life.** Scene 2 has him noticing the forge's weakened pulse. Scene 3 teaches the pattern. Scene 7 has him perform it, and his three-year burn-scar stops aching. That is *accumulation* — craft-witness as a skill with real consequences.

**Jarek's guilt.** He has not slept three years. He will not meet Grom's eyes. He is breaking apart under the weight of the half-kinship he carries. The adventure gives him multiple paths to confession and each one is a mercy.

**The murder-evidence redundancy (v1.3).** Three forms: cut beam + thunder-stone + destruction-plan oil-cloth. No single roll fragility. This is craft.

## What fails

**Read-aloud unevenness.** Scene 3's grave-stone description is gorgeous (*"a forge-master's tomb is a forge his family can kindle if they need light"*). Scene 2's shrine arrival is functional: *"The shrine sits in a hollow below a low cliff..."* It's information, not imagery. Scene 6's opening (Krun at the table with the coin on velvet) wants to be ceremonial and lands as stage-direction.

**Krun's initial voice is bureaucratic.** His opening speech at Scene 6 is three sentences of imperative tense. A man who has held fifty years of grievance should *sound* like he's held it. The module gives him good mechanics but doesn't give him the prose.

**Dungeon topology is linear.** Shrine → grave → shaft → Kruntharrak is a line with some branching. The space doesn't reward exploration or clever navigation.

**Single-point-of-failure on Hint 3 synthesis.** The nat-20 on Grom's Religion self-check is where Kessa locks it with Hint 2. If Grom rolls 19, synthesis waits for #0007.

**Krun's fate-branching is under-scaffolded.** Option A (no kinship) swings to Option C (Reorx-exile) on a Hint-3-unlocked path Grom may not have. The *emotional logic* of each branch needs clearer DM guidance.

**The module underplays Sigga's weight.** She carried the hammer three years. She knew the collapse was a murder. She *measured* Grom. But her *presence* — running Gavrek's Anvil, keeping the forge-fire, holding the ledger — is atmospheric, not mechanical.

## Specific recommendations

1. **Rewrite Scene 2 read-aloud.** Keep the structure. Lean into the *smell* — forge-heat and old iron and water-damp. Give Sigga one physical gesture when Grom dismounts that shows she's been waiting three years.

2. **Give Krun one internal monologue before he speaks.** Three sentences: *"He sits at the table facing the coin. Three years he's waited for this. Three years he's carried the thought 'Who did it?' with him to every meal. Today, finally, he will know."* Then the dialogue lands heavier.

3. **Add a branching note before Scene 6.** *"If Hint 3 (Reorx's Judgment) has landed, Grom knows (craft-witness) that grief-coils can be uncoiled. This knowledge changes how he sees Krun's fifty-year grievance."*

4. **Consider vertical dungeon-expansion for Scene 5.** What if the Lower Ore-Shaft has *multiple* levels? A second descent to a semi-collapsed lower chamber where Duren's destruction-plan forge-setup lives — that's where the evidence is *densest*, making the scene a mini-dungeon.

5. **Add a mechanical anchor for Sigga's measuring in Scene 2.** What if Grom passes a *Religion DC 10 self-check* to perform the hearth-greeting correctly? Success: Sigga *sees* Grom is real, and her Scene 3 confession lands with intimacy. Failure: she measures him anyway, but colder.

6. **Expand Jarek's surrender conditions.** Add a craft-witness path: *"If Grom invokes the inside-curve teaching, Jarek surrenders without a roll — 'You learned the inside-curve from Duren. You learned it real. I owe Duren that.'"* Makes Jarek's arc about craft-witness, not stats.

7. **Write the Voidmouth as an explicit DM advisory.** Give it a dedicated GM Notes entry: *"The Voidmouth is what kills lazy attuners. If a PC thinks they've won by emptying the coin and keeping it, they have lost. They do not know this until week 5 of play. This is intentional. Let them think they're clever. Let them find out."*

## Final line

The Wrath-Coin is a module for people who understand that treasures are *characters* — that Aelwen forged a thing that has its own moral opinion about who should hold it. One more pass on voice — make Krun sound like fifty years instead of six months — and it's shippable as-is.
