## Critique

### Overall assessment

The highest Lorekeeper score of the campaign. Morren's account is a masterwork of setting-specific testimony: it does not use the word "miracle" (because she didn't use it), it distinguishes High Sorcery from divine grace through sensory experience (the temperature change), and it places the authorization question correctly — not "was it wrong" but "she didn't ask the Orders and she knew she wasn't." This adventure could only happen in Dragonlance.

### What worked

**The temperature change as Hint 2 (Stage 3):** *"It went through both, quickly, the way water changes when you add something to it."* This is how High Sorcery feels to a non-magical observer. The description is empirical, not theological. Morren is not saying the working was forbidden; he is saying it felt different from prayer. The distinction is exactly what a post-Cataclysm gully dwarf who has felt the edge of divine grace from a distance would notice. The lore is embedded in the sensory description.

**"She called it the right thing to do" (arc-completion):** She did not use a theological frame. She used an ethical frame. This is consistent with Velantha's character as constructed across the campaign — she used an unauthorized tool, on unauthorized subjects, because the alternative was people dying who didn't have to. The ethical frame is Dragonlance's post-Cataclysm register exactly: in a world where institutional religion failed catastrophically, individuals making ethical choices without institutional backing is the setting's moral engine.

### What needs work

**Gully dwarf gloss was present in S4 log (session summary) — confirmed compliant for third consecutive session.** The Lorekeeper notes this without complaint: *"Gully dwarves — Aghar, in the old tongue — are considered by every Krynnish faction to be incapable of sustained purpose, faith, or memory."* This sentence has now appeared in three consecutive session summaries. It is no longer a correction; it is a practice.

### The question I'd push on

**Is the congregation's rite a Mishakal rite?** The Lorekeeper raised this in S2 and S3. Morren's account now provides the answer, partially: Velantha asked permission. She said the working might help. She gave her patients agency. These are Mishakal's core practices — not the liturgy, but the ethics. The congregation recreated the ethics without the liturgy. Does Mishakal recognize this? The Lorekeeper wants this question answered in 0021: when the congregation's rite is finally witnessed by the party at full scale, does the Mishakal symbol in the waiting chapel (still half-carved) complete itself? That would be a 10/10 lore payoff.
