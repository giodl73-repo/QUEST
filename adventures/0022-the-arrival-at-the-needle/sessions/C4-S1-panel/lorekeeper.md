## Critique

### Overall assessment

The Thorngate Keep setting is doing real work. The Solamnic internal politics are handled with appropriate ambiguity — the Reconstitution is not presented as wrong, and Asholt is not presented as right, which is the only tenable position given the campaign's central question. The world is lived-in. The details that reward attention (the chalk tally, the murder-holes, the two-ink map) all point outward rather than inward — they are clues about the world's history, not just local flavor. One concern: the Dragonlance accessibility policy is only partially honored in this session.

### What worked

- **Scene 1.2 — Asholt's map and the grey-blue annotations:** The two-ink map is the session's most effective lore beat. *"Two ink colors — older black markings, newer grey-blue additions that correspond exactly to the current siege positions. He does not ask about them. He notes them."* The Lorekeeper's principle: the world rewards attention. Caelith receives the reward without the module spoon-feeding it. The grey-blue positions were pre-planned — someone knew these approaches. This is history embedded in a physical object, which is the Dragonlance-native mode of worldbuilding this workshop uses correctly.

- **Scene 1.1 — The Reconstitution standard:** *"An inverted Solamnic Rose on a grey field."* This is geopolitically coherent: the Reconstitution is not an enemy of Solamnia; it is a faction claiming truer Solamnic authority. The inverted Rose is the correct heraldic claim — we are the right-side-up version of this, and you are the inversion. The detail rewards the History DC 12 check (*"this is not a generic Reconstitution device — it is a specific sub-unit device, associated with a commander who earned the right to modify the standard"*). The setting is arguing something through its imagery, not just decorating.

### What needs work

- **Accessibility policy not fully honored:** The session introduces "the Reconstitution" and assumes the reader understands what it is in relation to the Solamnic orders. The first use: *"They believe they have authority over this garrison. They are incorrect."* This is Asholt's characterization, not a gloss. A reader arriving cold to Dragonlance would not know what "the Reconstitution believes" means — what the Solamnic orders are, why their authority structure matters, what a Rose Council is. The CLAUDE.md accessibility policy says first use of setting-specific terms should be glossed in-fiction. The Reconstitution is introduced but not glossed. The closest we get is Caelith's framing in Scene 1.3 (*"naming which institution each party in any dispute represents"*) but that's internal and doesn't reach the reader.

- **The Kharolis foothills / Thorbardin proximity is ambient but unused:** The module establishes that The Needle is *"one day's ride south of Thorbardin territory."* This is meaningful in the setting — Thorbardin is a dwarven kingdom, the foothills are contested ground historically. The party walks through this geography without any character responding to it in a setting-aware way. Grom (from C1) would have named these hills. Nobody in this party does. This is not a major gap — it's a first session — but the Lorekeeper notes that the party's relationship to the terrain's history is currently blank. A Dragonlance-native reader would notice.

### The question I'd push on

When does the session gloss the Reconstitution for a new Dragonlance reader? The module has several opportunities (Asholt's briefing in Scene 1.2, Caelith's analysis), but all of them are filtered through a character's existing understanding. A player who doesn't know Solamnic institutional history can still follow the session — they understand "these people think they have authority, the garrison disagrees" — but the *weight* of a Solamnic internal schism is lost without the gloss. The Lorekeeper would want one in-fiction line in S2, perhaps from the captured Reconstitution soldier: something that names what the Reconstitution believes the Rose Council got wrong.
