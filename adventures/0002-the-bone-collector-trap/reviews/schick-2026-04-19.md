## Critique

I will say this plainly: the Bone-Collector's Reliquary is the best treasure this workshop has made, and by a real margin. The Silver Rose was good — a grief-object, a memory-eater, a moral test. But the Reliquary is *better because it inverts the Rose*. Same grief-economy, opposite direction. The Rose takes memories for vague minor benefits across many long rests. The Reliquary takes a lifetime's worth of memories in exchange for sixty seconds — one specific, terrible, vivid minute — of a person the bearer has already lost. The bargain is *exact*. The math is *clear*. A player who attunes knows precisely what they have traded and what they have gained.

This is what treasure-as-story means. Not that the treasure has lore. Every module has treasures with lore. But that the treasure *is itself the decision*, and the room it sits in is built to frame it. Your Vault does this. The shelf of five silver-bound skulls along the east wall, each a prior Minute held in reserve — that is the room that this reliquary deserves. The altar in the center. Keloth behind it. Pella bound to the iron box. I have seen the image in my head as cleanly as if I had drawn the map myself.

**The Varran-occupancy is the masterstroke.** You did not have to put Aelric's dead brother in this reliquary. You could have invented any dead Solamnic — a stranger the party could have chosen to sympathize with or not. Instead, you looked at Aelric's character sheet and saw the grief-paragraph. Varran. Of course. **The treasure was waiting for a specific player's character since the workshop opened.** That is the kind of long-wire design that shows through.

**The five shelf-reliquaries** are a clever design choice I almost missed. One of them holds Sergeant Hern, who died beside Varran. Four others are Nerakan. Each represents a future delivery arc. A future session where the party takes Hern's reliquary to his family in southern Abanasinia — I can see that module already. A future session where the party crosses into Neraka to return a stranger's last minute to a grieving mother — harder, richer, morally thornier. Your treasure is not a single artifact; it is **a shelf of future adventures**, each one anchored to a named dead.

**Pella as a non-treasure that is also a treasure.** This is delicate. She is a human child, and she must not be reduced to loot. But she functions in the session's emotional economy as a thing the players have custody of: they came for her; they leave with her; they decide what to do with her. I commend the module's discipline here — she is played as a person, her voice is specific, her decisions matter. The Pella-drawing-to-Memory-Echo mechanic does not repeat (we did that in S01); instead, her presence in the Vault is a *constraint* on Keloth's combat tactics and on the party's choices.

Where I would push harder:

**The reliquary's room does not yet fully express the reliquary's mechanic.** The Silver Rose's room had scored frescoes — the rose had been editing its own monument for a century. What has the Reliquary been doing to *its* room? I would add: Keloth's vault walls bear, in charcoal, a ledger — a list of names, decades long, each with a date and a location. The names of the dead whose reliquaries passed through this shelf. Some are crossed out (overwritten). Some are circled (given away). The ledger is the reliquary-work made visible. A party reading it understands, in a single sweep, what this shelf has *done* — and understands, too, that the reliquary in Aelric's hand is one of many, which makes the specific choice about Varran heavier, not lighter.

**The empty reliquary is underexplored.** You note it can hold any future dead if someone speaks a name at the moment of death within 60 feet. That is a tool the party might carry for the rest of the campaign. But you have not committed to what it *looks* like — is the silver duller? Is the skull newer, cleaner, or already cracked? I would make it visibly expectant: polished, eye-sockets open, silver thread at the ready. An artifact *waiting*.

**Keloth's own bracelet-reliquary** (his mother) is a beautiful detail. He has never attuned it. He cannot bring himself to. That is a single sentence of backstory doing the work of a chapter. I approve. The bracelet becomes a usable plot-hook for a future adventure — when the party is strong enough, and wise enough, to attune someone else's mother's last minute for purposes the DM cannot predict.

## Specific suggestions

1. **Add the wall-ledger in the Vault.** A charcoal list of names, decades long, crossed-out and circled per the reliquary's history. Grounds the shelf in the room's architecture.
2. **Commit to the empty reliquary's appearance.** Expectant; polished; eye-sockets open. Visible invitation.
3. **One of the shelf-reliquaries should have a woman's name** from a specific Nerakan village with a specific year of death. Prepare her as a hook for a future session. Right now the shelf-reliquaries are abstract; one with a specific identity would show players what the others could become.

## Final note

The treasure is doing the work this workshop was founded to do. The artifact is the story; the room frames the artifact; the decision lives in the player's character sheet. This is *White Plume Mountain* at a quieter scale — I mean that as a compliment. Keep making treasures like this.
