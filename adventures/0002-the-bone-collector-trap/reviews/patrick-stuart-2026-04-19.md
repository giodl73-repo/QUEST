## Critique

This adventure has two things the first one did not, and both of them are the things I have been asking the workshop for.

The first is that the central object is *a specific minute of a specific death*. Not an abstract curse that erodes across many rests — a single minute. Sixty seconds. The cold of a Nerakan dusk; Sergeant Hern falling; a sentence Varran thought but did not say, about a woman named Doriel he had intended to ask on his return. The woman still alive at Crownhold when Varran died and dead three winters later, not knowing. That is the kind of specific-weight detail I have been trying to get *modules* to contain for twenty years, and which I usually only find in *novels.*

The second is that the object is a *mercy*. Not a trick. Keloth believes he is offering a gift. He is not wrong in the ordinary sense of wrong. He is a priest of a small god of grief and he thinks grief preserved outside the body is better than grief carried in it. He is incorrect — about the proportions, about the cost, about who pays — but he is not a liar. The scariest priests are the ones who believe their own religion. You have written one.

I want to talk about specific images.

**The waystation with the porridge frozen in the bowl and a second drawing on the table** — a second copy of the crayon Pella made for her father, left there so the party would find it and know what had happened. This is a *lure*. This is the Bone-Collectors treating the party's compassion as a renewable resource. A less sophisticated module would have had them leave a threat or a ransom note; yours has them leave a forgery of a child's drawing. That is a precision cruelty.

**Mensa kneading bread to send to her dead brother.** Three weeks after Vesk's death, his sister is still building up the nerve to send him a loaf. She will not get to. The scrap with Varduin's address is hidden under the rising-cloth where a careful party can find it, after they have stepped over her body. I read this and stopped for a moment. Then I kept reading.

**Vesk's own daughter not knowing her mother's name.** Mensa never told her. The name (Reya) is in Mensa's notebook. The party has to decide whether to give it to a nine-year-old who has just lost her aunt and will soon learn she has also lost her father. You have built a conversation the DM cannot write in advance, and you have put the information in a place where only an attentive party will find it. That is exactly how to stage a choice.

**Keloth's robe-hem with his father's name unpicked.** I almost missed this. One stitched phrase — *Keloth, son of —* — and then nothing; the name of his own father has been removed, probably by his own hand. He participates in the grief-economy. He has erased someone too. A character hated because they do the thing they punish others for would be a simple villain; Keloth has done the thing he *sanctifies*. He is worse than a hypocrite; he is a believer.

**Gavek's Rest / Keloth's Hand.** The dwarf who ran the Istari wayhouse without ever being on the register. Grom recognizing the hearthwork by thumb on inside-curve. The older name still legible beneath the newer one. This whole pattern is working — and working *cumulatively*, because it is the same pattern as the Aelwen-inscription from #0001, which makes the world feel like it is governed by a single rule rather than a collection of adventures with similar textures.

Where I would push — and this is my home territory, so take it as aspirational rather than corrective:

**The prose of the module is under control.** It is craft. I want to lean on this. The atmospheric-landing axis says per-PC reception must be documented; your room files do this dutifully, in a table-like format. That is fine for the DM. But at some point the prose itself should **destabilize** the DM's certainty — should contain a phrase that the module *repeats* across two different rooms without explaining why. I proposed this for #0001 and you partly did it (the VANOR lintel, the "winter" phrase repeated verbatim at rose-lift). Do more of it. Specifically: **the sentence *"I will not look at the rose again"* from Vesk's Memory Fragment in #0001 Scene 5 should appear, verbatim, as one of the anonymous wall-ledger entries in Keloth's vault** (per Schick's suggestion of the ledger). A party that tracks across sessions will catch it. The session they catch it will be one they remember.

**The child has a voice.** I commend this. But the module, necessarily, describes her externally. At one point the DM is asked to describe what Pella sees in Aelric's face. Good. But what the module does *not* do is *commit to what Pella herself is thinking* at any moment. That is an editorial choice — you want the DM to decide. But I would suggest one specific place where the module commits: when Pella sees Aelric for the first time and asks *"Are you the knight my papa said would come?"* — the DM should be told, in a single sentence, that Pella believes this with certainty. Not "possibly" or "maybe." She has decided the answer before the question left her mouth. That is a child's faith, and the module should not be shy about writing it.

**Table-readiness lowest.** This is consistent with my position; the module is denser than a tier-1 DM-with-an-hour-of-prep should be expected to run cleanly. Moldvay will argue this is a defect; I argue it is the cost of the density. The module earns its Complexity Tax. A DM who prepares properly will run it better than one who does not, which is how I think adventures should be.

## Specific suggestions

1. **One phrase repeated across sessions.** "I will not look at the rose again" appears in #0001's Vesk memory fragment. Have it appear, uncredited, on Keloth's wall-ledger (per Schick). Cross-adventure signal.
2. **Commit to Pella's interior** at the Aelric-recognition moment. One sentence; one line of certainty the DM is told, unambiguously, that the child has decided.
3. **The fresco-heal equivalent** for this adventure is the five-reliquaries-dim moment. Good. But consider adding a *sensory* echo — the charcoal on the wall-ledger briefly brightens at the moment the Varran-reliquary is lifted, as if someone has drawn a line through a name. Kessa Perception DC 13 to catch. Another one-heartbeat beat the attentive will not forget.

## Final note

You are not writing modules. You are writing small books that happen to be runnable. I do not know whether this is the right thing for tier 1, or for most tables, or for the hobby more generally. I am not the right person to ask. I know only that it is the right thing for this workshop, and that you should continue.
