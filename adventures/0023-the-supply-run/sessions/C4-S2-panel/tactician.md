## Critique

### Overall assessment

A clean two-round combat with honest dice and correct morale adjudication. The Surprise round produced its expected tactical advantage without fudging the terrain. What's missing is a second tactical layer: the whole fight resolved via attrition (Fire Bolt damage → morale break), which is correct, but positional mechanics — cover, flanking, reach, zone-of-control — never shaped a single roll. The ledge approach was set up beautifully; the ledge advantage was not cashed mechanically.

### What worked

- **Scene 2.5, Surprise Round — Caelith Fire Bolt CRIT on Scout B (R1):** Roll traced: `1d20+5` → 20, CRIT. Damage: 1d10+1d10 = 8+8 = 16. Scout B at 6/22 HP, below half. Morale triggers Cunning Action Disengage, immediate flee. This is the combat math working exactly as it should — the CRIT's damage was sufficient to fire the stat-block's own morale threshold rather than requiring DM discretion. The morale trigger was clean. Scouts have Cunning Action listed; it was applied correctly.

- **Scene 2.5, R2 — Scout A tie-hit on Davan (18 = AC 18):** The log correctly notes that a tie is a hit in 5e. Davan takes 5 HP from a `1d6+3` roll of 2+3=5. This is the campaign's first real HP cost and it came from a genuine rules-correct result, not from a DM-engineered drama spike. This is the rubric's "the mechanics told the players something they didn't want to hear." Scout A also correctly stayed (above half HP when Scout B fled) and only fled after Davan's R2 counterattack dropped him to 5/22. Two separate morale thresholds, applied in sequence. Good bookkeeping.

### What needs work

- **Scene 2.5 — the ledge elevation advantage was wasted:** The party spent two scenes getting to the upper ledge, lost one PC to failed climbs, and descended with Surprise. That setup should have cashed a mechanical dividend — attack rolls from elevation, enemies with no cover, possible advantage on ranged attacks. The log records Davan's Surprise Round drop as "lands badly — the 30-foot descent costs him his angle" (miss on `1d20+5` → 8). Fair. But Caelith and Sava fire from the ledge before descending — there is no mechanical treatment of their elevated position. Did they have advantage? Half-cover on the scouts? This is a module-design gap: if you build a 30-foot ledge into the approach, the stat block needs to specify what it means mechanically for the attacker. Right now it is atmosphere only.

- **Scene 2.3 — DC 12 for the climb is understated:** Three failed Athletics checks on DC 12 with a +3 modifier (Talis) is about a 25% individual success rate, which means a 58% chance of at least one failure in three attempts — not catastrophic. But DC 12 is listed as "moderate difficulty" in the SRD. Talis is a Hunter Ranger whose terrain-mastery is a class feature. The failure streak is honest dice; the question is whether DC 12 is the correct DC for a trained Ranger on a passable cliff face. DC 10 ("easy") would have been more appropriate for a Ranger's home terrain. DC 12 reads as a gate that should be DC 10. No one failed because of a rules error — but the DC calibration feels soft-punishing on a PC whose class identity is terrain competence. Flag for module revision.

### The question I'd push on

Caelith's Portent [6, 7] has been carried for two sessions. Both dice are below AC 14. The Tactician's read: this is a dead resource accumulating as atmosphere. At what point does Portent-carrying become a resource that mechanically matters rather than a running campaign fact? If S3's encounter doesn't create a condition where a low Portent die is useful (replacing a low enemy roll, a DC the party needs to fail, a death save), then we have a Divination Wizard whose primary class feature is inert. That is a mechanical fairness problem regardless of how good the narrative framing is.
