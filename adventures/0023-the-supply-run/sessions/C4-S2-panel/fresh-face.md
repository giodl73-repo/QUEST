## Critique

### Overall assessment

This is the most legible session of the campaign so far. The situation is clear (cart is late, supplies are low, go find it), the stakes are translated into fiction (five days crossed to five in the ledger, not "your supply die drops"), and every dice roll has a visible reason. A new player can follow all of it. My main concern is Caelith — specifically his Portent dice, which are mentioned twice and never explained, and his note-taking, which is treated as characteristic detail without grounding what he is or what the notes are for. Caelith is the least accessible PC to a new reader, and he also has the session's most important NPC interaction.

### What worked

- **Scene 2.1 — the ledger as exposition device:** *"The cart is four days late. I have five days of materials left."* This is perfect accessibility. A new player does not need to know what the supply die is. They do not need to know what the session's stakes structure is. They see a number in a ledger (five days) and a shortfall (four days late) and they understand immediately: we are one delay away from a problem that will hurt people. No Dragonlance vocabulary required. No D&D vocabulary required. The fiction does all the work. This is how the setting's military-supply context should always be introduced.

- **Scene 2.5, Round 2 — Scout A hits Davan on a tie:** *"The longsword catches Davan across the upper arm — a clean cut, the first real damage of the campaign. Davan takes 5 HP. Davan: 23/28 HP. He does not react visibly."* The log translates the mechanical outcome into physical reality (the cut, the specific location) and tells us something characterological (he does not react visibly) in the same sentence. A new player would understand: Davan was hurt; Davan does not show it. That is characterization without explanation. The HP numbers are visible but they are framed by the fiction, not leading it. The rule about ties (tie = hit in 5e) is noted in the log for DM reference without interrupting the narrative. Correctly handled.

### What needs work

- **Caelith's Portent dice: mentioned but never explained:** Scene 2.7: *"Caelith, without looking up: 'That's twice they've logged us.' He adds a note to his book."* The note-taking is characterization. Appropriate. But earlier in the session summary: *"Caelith Portent [6, 7] CARRIED."* In the party state table. A new player reading this log does not know what Portent is. The mechanics section does not explain it. The previous session presumably established it, but this session treats it as a running campaign fact without any in-fiction translation. What does Caelith's Portent mean in fiction? Does he see the numbers? Dream them? The log never tells us. A new player is left with a mystery that the experienced player recognizes as a class feature. The session should have one line — even in DM narration — that translates what Portent means in Caelith's experience.

- **The Reconstitution: who are they, why should I care?** Scene 2.4: *"Garrison build, Reconstitution's grey-field device on his collar."* A new player at this table knows the party is at a Solamnic garrison (established in S1, presumably). They do not know what the Reconstitution is, what they want, or why they are at this pass. Pren's H1 line — *"She came back for something that wasn't finished. Not the pass"* — is the most important piece of information the session delivers, and it is addressed to a player who already has context for who "she" might be. A new reader of the log does not have that context. One line of in-fiction orientation would fix this: something Pren says or doesn't say that signals the faction's basic shape without requiring a lore lecture.

### The question I'd push on

*"He is stable. He doesn't need to be stabilized — he's above 0. He needs to be brought back from 1."* — This is DM-internal voice explaining a mechanical distinction (stable vs. 0 HP vs. 1 HP) that a new player might not parse without a 5e rules reference. The session then says *"She casts Cure Wounds"* and the logic is: Sava chose to heal someone she didn't have to. That is the arc beat. A new player should understand that choice clearly. Right now, the distinction between "he will not die" and "Sava chose to heal him anyway" is buried in HP notation that requires rules literacy to read. The fiction should carry that distinction — something like: *"He was breathing on his own. He would survive the walk back. Sava healed him anyway."* One sentence. The arc beat lands cleanly without the HP apparatus.
