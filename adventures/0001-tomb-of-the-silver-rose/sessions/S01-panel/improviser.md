## Critique

### Overall

The DM said yes to the important things, said no to almost nothing, and found a third answer for the ambiguous things. This is a good session for the Improviser lens because the module left real holes the party stepped into, and the DM filled those holes fairly rather than by funnel. Twelve `<!-- SURPRISE -->` tags is a healthy count for a tier-1 one-shot.

### What worked

- **Grom's Pella-drawing-to-Memory-Echo interaction.** (Scene 8.) The module's Echo text says *"a character who presents her with the Pella-drawing and says a brief thing aloud about remembering"* triggers the second line. It does NOT specify who would do this or what they'd say. The module left a hole the size of a player-choice. Grom filled it with his Reorx-rite register. The DM rewarded with the Echo's second line. This is yes-and at its best: the module seeded the opportunity; the PC brought the voice; the DM honored both.
- **Kessa's "catalog vs. test" d6 moment.** (Scene 3, bowl ward.) Her "in doubt" heuristic says 1-3 catalog, 4-6 test. She rolled `1d20+5` at `total=10`; I note she didn't roll a d6 here because she simply cataloged by default after failing to identify. This is a heuristic-driven fall-back, and the DM let it be. Good improvisational respect for the PC sheet.
- **Aelric speaking the Oath aloud at the R05 door.** (Scene 5.) The module provides this as a "roleplay beat; no mechanical effect." Grom noted the door's settling. Aelric said nothing further. Four sentences of improvised silence; the DM did not force more. Restraint is improvisation, too.
- **Thera's grief-chain-reaction.** (Scene 8, Pella's drawing.) No check, no save. The DM let the fiction's density do the work. *"The smell of the savory-and-rye bread her mother had baked, twenty-three years ago, came into the room — briefly, impossibly — and went."* This is the DM saying yes to something no player rolled for.

### What needs work

- **The Wandering-outcome-4 collapse specification.** (Scene 6.) The DM chose "R02 east archway collapses" because it was narratively useful — forcing the party up via R05's stair. That's good adjudication, but the module leaves this "DM choice." A future version should roll d3 or d4 among specific possible corridors so that the collapse is honest rather than narratively convenient. The Improviser trusts dice; the DM currently has permission to pick.
- **Brother Laen's behavior matrix tilted toward the expected outcome.** (Scene 9.) The party had Aelwen's Letter. Laen's matrix says "Offer 300 gp." The DM ran the scene exactly at 300. But a truly improvisational DM would have probed: what if the party hadn't mentioned the letter until late? What if Grom had raised his doubt aloud? The matrix worked; but the DM played it on-script. That's module-fidelity, not improvisation.
- **The "try Thera's shortsword in the slot" beat was skipped.** (Scene 6.) The module's Option-B text explicitly mentions that "without the ritualistic fit of the notched longsword, it still triggers the Armor." The party didn't try. The DM didn't prompt. A player might have considered "will any sword work?" — and if the DM had been truly improvisational, they might have set up a moment where Thera almost tried and Kessa stopped her. This is a small missed hook for player-discovery.

### The question I'd push on

**How much of the session's emergent feel came from the module's scaffolding, and how much came from the DM's flexibility?** The module is rich; the party heuristics are specific; the dice were honest. The DM had a lot of permission to let the fiction breathe without inventing. That means this session is a *generous test* of the playtest-as-improvisation lens. A session in a flatter module, or with a less-developed party, would test the DM's improvisation much more. I want S02 to push on a space the module didn't anticipate — an NPC the party decides to recruit; a path the module closes that the party opens with a clever slot; a rule the module is silent on that the party tests.
