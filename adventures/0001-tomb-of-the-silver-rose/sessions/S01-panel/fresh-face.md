## Critique

### Overall

A dense session, and a dense one to follow. A new player at this table would have been carried by the DM's in-fiction exposition and by Kessa's running commentary, which is essentially in-game Wikipedia. But the session passed: a new player could have read the log and understood 80%+ of what happened and why. That's the Fresh Face test, and it held — just.

### What worked

- **Mechanics translated into fiction.** "The cold settled into the joints of his plate" (Scene 4, Aelric's exhaustion). Not "Aelric takes 1 level of exhaustion." The DM translated the mechanic into what the character would feel. A new player reading this would know *something has happened* even if they don't yet know the mechanical word for it.
- **DCs described as in-fiction difficulty.** "The capstone was heavy; it took both of them" (Scene 2). Cooperative Athletics succeeded; the narration communicated it as physical reality, not as a dice outcome. New-player-friendly.
- **Kessa's scholarly voice doubles as exposition.** Because Kessa catalogs everything, a new player gets the session's in-fiction explanations in her voice rather than the DM's. When Kessa says *"A rose of moon-silver made provincially carries the kind of resonance the Test does not name"* (Scene 1), she is explaining Dragonlance's magic-regulation system without the DM stopping the session to explain it.
- **Brother Laen explains the Rose Council without lecturing.** His diffidence itself IS the explanation — a new player sees a nervous clerk from an Order that would like this errand to go away, and the setting's institutional ambiguity comes across through his behavior, not through a monologue.
- **Party files' Playstyle Heuristics are legible.** (Not in the session log, but in the artifacts a new player would open.) The "decision order" and "when in doubt d6" format translates playstyle to a readable recipe. A new player assigned any of these four PCs could play them with 20 minutes of preparation.

### What needs work

- **"Solamnic" is used 37 times without ever being in-fiction-defined.** The session assumes readers know the Knights of Solamnia are the Dragonlance chivalric order. A new player at a cold table would not know this. The first time "Solamnic" appears in Scene 1, a Brother Laen description — *"a Knight of the Crown, the junior order of Solamnia's chivalric knighthood"* — would have cost nothing and welcomed everyone.
- **The Oath is quoted in Solamnic: "Est Sularus oth Mithas."** The session never translates it. Aelric speaks it with authority; the tomb has it over the R05 door; the party respects it. But "my honor is my life" — the translation — appears nowhere in the session log. A new player would have to infer.
- **Kessa's Portent is mentioned but its mechanics are not explained.** Scene 1's prep includes "Portent pre-rolls (to be rolled at session open)." The rolls landed (13 and 19), were never used, and the reader never learns what Portent IS. A new player reading the log sees a mechanic-in-frontmatter that never surfaces. Either explain or cut.
- **The Cold Pulse / Wandering tables are rolled on schedule, and the fiction responds, but the schedule is invisible to a new player.** A player at the table would notice "the DM rolled something" and wonder. The log captures the rolls faithfully, but a new-player-facing explanation of *why* the DM is rolling d6s periodically would help.
- **"VANOR" and its significance.** Thera reads it as a misspelling of "Vaenor." The significance is the tomb forgetting its own name. The session log has this clearly for a reader who's paying attention, but a new player may not catch *why* it matters until Scene 8's attribution-drift reveal. A one-sentence tag on first encounter — *"The tomb appears to be losing its memory of itself"* — would help new readers without spoiling.

### The question I'd push on

**Is the workshop designing for experienced D&D readers, or for readers who might be encountering the setting through these sessions?** Because the answer shapes the prose. Session logs designed for experienced readers can let terms like "Solamnic," "Portent," and "Test of High Sorcery" float without definition; the readers will have them. Session logs designed for readers new-to-Dragonlance need a glossary or in-fiction gloss pattern. Both are valid; the workshop has not yet declared which it is. S02 should pick.
