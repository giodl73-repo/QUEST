# Room 06 — The Sarcophagus Chamber

## Read-aloud
> An octagon, twenty feet across. In the center, a sarcophagus of pale stone, carved as a knight in repose — hands crossed on the breastplate. A silver rose is set into the breastplate between her hands, petals folded shut. Frescoes cover the walls — a life, in panels — and each panel is scored across, the same pattern, again and again.

## Features

- **The sarcophagus (central, raised one step).** 8 ft long, pale limestone. Carved figure of Sir Ilendra Vaenor in Knight of the Rose plate. Her stone hands are crossed on her breastplate; between them — set into the stone — is the **Silver Rose of Vaenor**, petals folded shut. The rose is real; the rest is carving. Ilendra's stone hand (the right one) is closed in a fist around nothing — a close look reveals a recessed slot in the fist, sized precisely to take a sword-tip.
- **The frescoes (walls, all eight panels).** Ilendra's life:
  1. Childhood, with her brother Caen.
  2. Caen's cadet ceremony.
  3. The Third Dragon War — Caen's death in battle. *Caption beneath, chiseled into the base:* **"We do not carry the dead within us. We carry the shape of ourselves against their absence."** — uncredited.
  4. Ilendra receiving the rose from the silversmith Aelwen (her face blank here — *this panel is not defaced*).
  5. Ilendra's Rose Knight induction.
  6. Ilendra at a funeral — her own parents, years later, aged beyond her. *Faces scored away.*
  7. Ilendra alone in the rose-gardens of Vaenshold.
  8. Ilendra's own interment.
  Every panel **except #4** has been scored across by a fine-edged tool, in patterns shaped like rose-petals. DC 13 Investigation connects the marks to the rose directly. (A party that noted Room 04's Book II margin-quote will realize Panel 3's caption is *the same sentence* Ilendra wrote "as Aelwen said to my father" — the rose has taken Aelwen's name from one of the two.)
- **The air.** Colder than Rooms 02-05. Still dry.
- **The silence.** Absolute. Even weapons drawn make no sound for a moment, as if the room refused to hear them, before normal sound returns.

### Retrieving the Rose — Three Options

The Silver Rose is set into the stone breastplate between Ilendra's hands. Her right stone fist has a hidden slot sized for a sword-tip. To take the rose:

- **Option A — Brute.** **Strength (Athletics) DC 15** to lever open the sarcophagus lid. Noisy — the lid grinding stone-on-stone. **Triggers the Armor.** Inside: Ilendra's remains, armored; a silver pendant (40 gp, not cursed — see minor-loot).
- **Option B — The Notched Longsword Key.** If the party has the notched longsword from Room 03, the notch fits the hidden slot in Ilendra's stone fist precisely. Inserting and turning it: the breastplate-clasp releases silently. The rose comes free with almost no sound. **The Armor does NOT animate.** This is the reward for thorough exploration.
- **Option C — Smash.** Strength (Athletics) DC 18 with a heavy weapon to break the breastplate carving. Loud and destructive. **Triggers the Armor AND the Memory Echo appears mid-combat** (rather than after).

*(Note: a party without the notched sword but with a clever idea about the stone fist's slot can use any sword or similar-shaped object: treat as Option A — DC 15 Investigation to notice the slot + a sword-tip fit — but *without* the ritualistic fit of the notched longsword, it still triggers the Armor, because the rose does not accept improvisation as respect.)*

## Encounter — Ilendra's Armor *(triggered by Options A or C, not B)*

**See `encounters/06-ilendras-armor.md` for the full stat block and tactics.**

1 × Animated Armor (SRD, CR 1, 200 XP). AC 18, HP 33. Multiattack: 2 slam attacks (+4, 1d6+2 bludg).

Fights with cold deliberation; priority = whoever is closest to the Rose (or carrying it). Does not pursue out of the room. At 0 HP sags to a seated position against the east wall and ceases.

### The Memory Echo

When the Rose is taken from its setting — *whether before the Armor fight (Option B), during (Option A lull), or after (either option)* — a translucent figure of Sir Ilendra at age fifty appears briefly by the sarcophagus. She is not an adversary. She has no HP.

She speaks one sentence, softly and clearly. The room hushes for her:

> *"Tell my brother I did not forget."*

Then she fades.

**Interaction rules** (non-statted entity; per Moldvay gate):
- She cannot be touched. A hand passes through her.
- She does not answer questions *about the rose.* She answers only *"my brother."*
- A character who speaks the name **Caen** aloud in her presence causes her to weep silver light for a single heartbeat — not grief, *recognition.* No mechanical effect. Ask the player what it was like.
- A character who presents her with the **Pella-drawing** from Room 05 (Vesk's daughter) and says a brief thing aloud about remembering causes her to turn her head toward the drawing and, for the only time, *smile.* She says a second sentence, once: *"Then someone is still remembering."* Then she fades as usual. This is a gift for players who paid attention in Room 05.
- If the Armor is still fighting, she appears regardless. The room hushes for her sentence even mid-combat. The Armor, if fighting, pauses a beat — as if listening — and then resumes.

**Wisdom (Insight) DC 12:** Ilendra does not remember that her brother is dead.

### The Reaction Moment — the Fresco Heals

At the moment the Rose is lifted from its setting (any option), **Panel 6 of the frescoes** (Ilendra at her parents' funeral, faces previously scored away) *heals.* The scoring ripples backward like frost retreating; for **one heartbeat**, the faces are visible — a man and a woman, aged, both with Ilendra's cheekbones. Then the scoring re-etches itself, smooth and inevitable, and the faces are gone again.

Any character watching the panels at that moment may, with a DC 12 Wisdom (Perception) check, glimpse the faces clearly enough to describe them — the DM asks the player what the parents looked like, and the player answers. Write it down. (Ilendra had forgotten her own parents' faces. The rose has returned them, briefly, because taking the rose loosens its grip. They belong to the player now.)

**Simultaneously**, Pan 3's caption *("We do not carry the dead within us...")* briefly reacquires its attribution: for the same heartbeat, the line *— Aelwen* is visible at the caption's end. Then it fades. Aelwen's name has been returned to the stone for a second, a century after the rose took it.

### The Repeated Phrase

At the instant the Rose is lifted, the DM says — softly, without commentary, as narration and not in any character's voice:

> *"And the winter was colder than any I have known."*

If any player reacts — if they recognize the phrase from Room 02 — the DM does not confirm. Just keeps going. The repetition is the symptom; the DM is a vector for the tomb's forgetting, not its interpreter.

## GM Notes
- When the party enters the room, give them a moment *before* the fight. Let them see the frescoes. Let them understand what they are standing in.
- The Memory Echo is a gift to the players, not a combat element. The Pella-drawing unlocks a second line from her — do not volunteer this; it's a reward for remembering Room 05.
- The fresco healing is a *single heartbeat.* If the DM narrates it slowly, it's not a heartbeat; it's a scene. Narrate it fast — one breath — and let players who caught it feel they caught something.
- **Option B (the Notched Longsword key)** is the reward for thorough exploration. Do not signpost. Let the players discover the notch fits the slot in Ilendra's stone fist.
- If the party tries to **leave the rose in place and go**: let them. Brother Laen will not be happy. Adventure arc branches here.
- When they leave the tomb with the rose, describe the wind outside as suddenly warm — unseasonably so, almost welcoming. The cairn has finished its work.

### Memory Fragment (optional — read only if the party lingers)
> *Sir Ilendra, at twenty-six, when the rose first touched her palm: "It was cold, and it fit. I understood, for a moment, that if I held it I would not have to carry what I had been carrying. I did not yet know that what I had been carrying was who I had loved, and what I would be given instead would be who she had loved, and that a century later I would not know the difference. I would know only that there had been grief, and that grief was mine now, and that I could go on."*
