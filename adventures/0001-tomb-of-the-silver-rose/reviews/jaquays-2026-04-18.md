## Critique

Let me start with what this dungeon does right, because it does several things right that most published tier-1 modules ignore.

The topology has a loop. Room 2 connects east and west to Rooms 3 and 4; Rooms 3 and 4 both descend to Room 5; Room 5 connects north to Room 6. That gives the party a choice on the way down (fight or puzzle) and a *different* choice on the way back up (they can return via whichever path they didn't take). You have a Z-axis connection (the shaft) in addition to a standard staircase. You have a shortcut that unlocks through play — the shaft opens only after the Scriptorium puzzle is solved. These are all correct choices.

The shortcut is the single smartest design move in this dungeon. A party that solves Room 04 gets a reward: fast descent to the final room, skipping the Pillar Hall's attendants entirely. That is exactly the kind of earned shortcut I build my levels around. The party should feel that *their thoroughness bought them something.*

But — and there is a but — six rooms is a small canvas on which to paint a proper living dungeon. At six rooms, I can *hold* the whole map in my head after one read. That is good for the players (they can navigate). It is less good for a designer trying to make a *place.* My own *Caverns of Thracia* is more than seventy rooms across multiple levels, and the reason is not padding; it is that a dungeon with enough space to *explore* — to find dead ends, to find things you didn't need the first time, to come back later and realize Room 20 connects to Room 7 by a route you missed — has a different emotional register than a dungeon where you can clear every keyed area in one session. That is not a criticism of this module, which is explicitly scoped as a one-session adventure; it is a reminder that the techniques you've deployed here will scale, and that dungeon #0002 should grow.

The **inhabitants are static.** This is my main architectural objection. The attendants in Room 03 rise and fall based purely on whether the party crosses the centerline; they have no patrol route, no reaction to distant noise. The ghoul in Room 05 is strictly conditional on prior loudness, not on ongoing party behavior. The Animated Armor in Room 06 is a pure switch. In a Jaquaysed dungeon, the *inhabitants move* — the armor might shift position between visits, the attendants might leave bone-trails between the pillars, the rose itself might draw cold deeper into the chambers over hours. Right now the tomb is a diorama that animates when triggered. A living tomb would be reshaping itself while the party works.

Still: the fact that the party can retreat from Room 03 and the skeletons will *stop at the centerline* is a good use of asymmetric behavior. That is the kind of thing that turns a fight into a navigation decision. I approve.

The secret-that-isn't-really-secret problem: Room 04 is invisible to the party unless they happen to try the west archway. That archway has no draw. A Jaquaysed dungeon gives the players a *reason* to take each branch — a sound from behind one door, a draft, a scrape. Right now both archways in Room 02 are identical in presentation, and the west one leads to the more interesting room. Fifty percent of parties will skip the best room.

The Memory Echo in Room 06 is a beautiful piece of geography-as-story. Ilendra appears *by the sarcophagus* — not hovering, not swooping — because that is where her memory is located. A ghost bound to a specific coordinate is the kind of detail that makes a dungeon feel like a place.

## Specific suggestions

1. **Give Room 02 a cue that distinguishes the west archway.** A faint draft carrying the smell of dried vellum; a fragment of an old Solamnic hymn on the lintel; the wind from outside sighing slightly louder from one direction. Reward the curious without railroading.
2. **Make the attendants patrol.** Once the encounter begins, skeletons should shift position between the pillar-rows even if the party retreats. If the party returns, they find bones in different places. Living dungeons notice the intruders.
3. **Consider a second vertical element.** Room 02's weeping wall could have a crack wide enough to peer into — revealing Room 05's ceiling shaft from an unexpected angle. Not a shortcut; a *glimpse.* Players who peer in can map the dungeon before descending.

## Final note

This dungeon Jaquays reasonably well at the scale you've chosen. The loop is real, the shortcut is earned, the verticality exists. For a six-room dungeon, this is about as much non-linear space as you can honestly offer. When dungeon #0002 scales up, use what you've learned here and push farther — more loops, more vertical connections, inhabitants that *act* rather than *wait.*
