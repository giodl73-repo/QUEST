## Critique

There is a good object in this dungeon. A rose of moon-silver, petals hinged, made by a woman who started forgetting her own daughter's face while she was making it, given to a father who killed himself after giving it to his daughter, buried with a woman who stopped aging at fifty and kept a written list of the living that grew longer every year until she could not remember the people whose names were on it. That is a real object. I can feel the weight of it. I can feel the weight of the Baron's cousin, somewhere in the world, receiving word that someone has taken the rose from the cairn, and doing nothing about it because she does not yet know whether the rose is lost for good or merely moved. I can feel Aelwen's hands.

The rose is doing the work. Everything else in the dungeon is arranged around it — the pillars scored from inside, the frescoes scored from inside, the mosaic with the face chiseled away. The tomb has been *editing itself* for a century. That is a quiet and terrible image. I want the players to understand it. I think they will.

But here is where I am going to press, because I think you have written a good dungeon and I think it is *almost* a dungeon that knows what it is about.

The tomb is losing its memory of itself *in the abstract.* The rose has scored panels in Room 03 and Room 06; the pillars are defaced; there are references to the tomb being "edited." These are visual symptoms. **They are not sensory symptoms the way the rose's curse in a living bearer is sensory.** When the rose erodes a bearer's memory, the mechanics say the bearer *loses a memory of a loved one and replaces it with a fragment of Ilendra's grief.* That is specific. That is concrete.

What is the equivalent for the tomb? **The tomb should be losing its memory of itself in ways the players can feel.** A phrase the DM is instructed to repeat four times in different rooms without realizing it. A carving in Room 03 whose caption the DM read earlier in Room 02, but worded slightly differently. A name on the lintel of Room 05 that should be *Vaenor* but is written as *Vanor* — is it a typo? No. It's the tomb forgetting. Room 04's philosophical book quotes a passage about memory; Room 06's frescoes illustrate that passage — but the passage in Room 04 is attributed to Aelwen, and the passage in Room 06 is attributed to nobody. The tomb has forgotten Aelwen.

The players may never catch these. That is correct. That is what atmospheric design is. The tomb is *having an experience* and the players are walking through it. If they notice, they notice. If they don't, the dungeon is still behaving that way, and the weather of it touches them without being explicit.

I want to praise specifically the **Antechamber of Tears.** That is a room I recognize. A bowl that never overflows; a wall that weeps at the pace of tears, not of groundwater; a fragment of a woman's voice caught on a failed Wisdom save. *"... and the winter was colder than any I have known ..."* — ask the player to write the fragment down. That instruction is beautiful. It is the sort of thing a GM forgets to do under time pressure, but it is exactly the sort of thing that makes a session a thing the players will remember a year later.

But you are letting me off the hook too often elsewhere. The prose is *under control,* which is craft, but it is also sometimes too clean. Room 05's read-aloud — "a dead brazier stands by a single oaken door" — is accurate but it is not surprising. I want to be *caught* by a word. The weeping wall is working. The frescoes are working. The rest of the prose wants the same strangeness.

The table-readiness is strong. That is a virtue. My own books are famously hard to run and I accept the critique. But I will say, as a small craft note, that sometimes the DM cheatsheet is doing the work the prose should be doing. When the cheatsheet says *"If the party kills Brother Laen... the entire Order becomes an enemy,"* I want the prose of the module to have made the reader feel that *before* they reach the cheatsheet. Not as a rule, but as a weight.

## Specific suggestions

1. **Have the tomb forget itself in small textual ways.** Rename Vaenor as Vanor in one inscription. Attribute a passage in Room 04 to Aelwen, but in Room 06 have the same passage appear anonymously. Repeat a phrase from Room 02's read-aloud at the moment the rose is taken in Room 06 — the DM repeats it without commentary. Let the players feel it before they understand it.
2. **Write one sentence per room that is a *memory fragment* from a prior bearer.** In Room 01, a line about the wind from Aelwen's perspective. In Room 04, the Baron's last thought as he hands the rose to Ilendra. In Room 06, the first sentence Ilendra thought on attuning. These are one-line inclusions the DM reads only if the players linger — *optional,* not required. Gifts to the slow party.
3. **Let Room 05's desiccated corpse be *the Bone-Collector who remembered his own daughter's face at the end.*** Give him a name. Give him one sentence of backstory. The tomb has taken his memory from him; he has chosen to remember her even if it kills him. He has gnawed through his own arm to leave her name scratched into the brazier-ash with blood. The party can read it before the ghoul or the petal distract them. That is the kind of specificity that earns a room.

## Final note

This is the treasure-story of a minor knight who forgot her brother so that she could go on. Write it as that. The tomb is Ilendra, not a setting for her. Lean further into the elegy. The mechanics are in place. The atmosphere is in place. What remains is confidence — trusting that the tomb is enough of a character that you can *make the reader feel* its confusion, not just tell them about it.
