## Critique

### Overall assessment

A new player could have followed this session. The dice are visible. The mechanics are translated. The fiction does the explaining. My two concerns are specific: "gully dwarves" as a term goes to work without introduction, and "Mishakal" appears as if the reader should already know her. Neither is catastrophic — both are fixable with one sentence each. The session is the most accessible first session this workshop has produced.

### What worked

- **Scene 3 (Awakened Mind translation):** *"He uses Awakened Mind to reach Mig. He says, in the space between her ears: 'We were sent to assess the building. We are not here to drive you out.'"* A new player reading this knows immediately what Awakened Mind does — it lets you talk to someone inside their mind. No mechanical terminology. No "as a bonus action." The log writes it as a character action and trusts the reader to understand. This is the session's best in-fiction mechanic translation. The Improviser loves it for the improvisation; the Fresh Face loves it for the accessibility.

- **Dice lines throughout:** The 🎲 format with `rolls=[N]`, `mod=+M`, `total=T` is legible to a reader who has never played D&D. The combat in Scene 2.2 is followable: you can read "Maret attack total=19 vs AC 12 = HIT, damage 1" without knowing what a d20 is. The consequences are always stated. Every roll leads immediately to a narrated result. A new player could read the Ghoul fight and understand everything that happened.

### What needs work

- **"Gully dwarves" without introduction:** The session log opens with *"found it occupied by seven gully dwarves — Velantha's congregation."* A new player who knows nothing about Dragonlance reads this as "small creature similar to gnomes or halflings." They do not read it as "the creatures every faction in Krynn has agreed are incapable of faith, memory, or sustained purpose." The log's emotional payoff — a congregation that has maintained daily devotion for thirty years — is extraordinary only if you know why it shouldn't exist. Without the Aghar gloss, a fresh reader thinks: "Some small folk live here. Okay." With the gloss, they think: "The creature everyone said couldn't do this has been doing it for thirty years." One sentence is the difference between a surprising revelation and background furniture.

- **"Mishakal" unglosed:** Scene 5 names Mishakal's symbol as "Mishakal's symbol" without explaining who Mishakal is. The whole cathedral is being built for her. A fresh reader following the session doesn't yet know that the patron deity of this entire construction project is the goddess of healing — which makes Velantha's use of High Sorcery to heal people doubly ironic. One sentence: "Mishakal's mark — the blue infinity, the sign of Krynn's goddess of healing" — is enough.

### The question I'd push on

**Will the V.S. mystery hold for a new player through multiple sessions?** Renn found initials scratched into the satchel. The log records this as a meaningful beat. But a new reader doesn't yet know whose initials these are, and the log doesn't hint at it. In C1, the workshop threaded mysteries across sessions successfully (Kessa's commonplace book, the silver rose's provenance). For a new player to stay invested in V.S., they need *one more signal* in S2 — something that makes V.S. feel active, not just archived. A conversation where Mig uses a word or a gesture that connects to the initials would be enough. Don't let the detail go cold between sessions.
