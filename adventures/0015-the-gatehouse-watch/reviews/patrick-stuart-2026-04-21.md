## Verdict: PASS

"The haunting is not a haunting." That sentence, in the background section, is the entire adventure in six words. The workshop has been producing increasingly good adventure design; this is the first adventure where the premise's central image is genuinely strange. Gully dwarves performing an unauthorized vigil in a half-built cathedral for thirty years — doing what they could without knowing what they were doing — is something that could only happen in this world, with this setting's particular attitude toward who gets divine attention and who doesn't.

## Strongest axes

**Premise (9/10):** The emotional logic is specific: Velantha healed people the institutions had decided weren't worth healing, and those people have been faithful to her memory in the one way available to them. The party arrives expecting an empty building and finds thirty years of unauthorized devotion. The inversion is clean and earned. Counter-evidence: the logline buries the twist — "gully dwarves, confused and scared, who have come here because a healer told them this place would matter someday" undersells it. The twist deserves one more word: *they came back.*

**Lore (8/10):** This adventure can only happen in Dragonlance. Specifically: in a world where gully dwarves are considered beneath divine notice, where High Sorcery has a prohibition on healing non-humanoids, where the Orders would classify what Velantha did as a violation rather than a miracle. Every element of the premise depends on the setting's specific hierarchy of who matters. The relic's property (warmest in the hands of people who are afraid and helping anyway) is Dragonlance distilled to a sentence. Counter-evidence: the term "gully dwarves" appears without a gloss in the background section. New Dragonlance readers won't know they're the lowest-status creature in this world's social hierarchy — that context is what makes their thirty-year vigil extraordinary rather than merely odd.

**Curse / Relic (7/10):** The satchel's Presence/Desire section is doing work. "When set down in a corner, it seems somehow closer to the room's center the next time the holder looks." That's exactly the right register — not a mechanical effect, a temperament. The tally stone (30 marks scratched into the wall-walk parapet) is the adventure's best non-artifact object. It is evidence of time without explanation. Counter-evidence: the Memory Fragments are all from Velantha's perspective. The congregation has been here thirty years and has no Memory Fragments. Dova (the First-Keeper, who was here before anyone) has no presence in the dungeon at all.

## Weakest axes

**Dungeon (7/10):** R5 (the Waiting Chapel) is perfect. The half-carved Mishakal symbol ending mid-stroke is the image that will stay with players. The child-sized handprint in tallow is domestic in exactly the right way — it is not threatening; it is evidence that someone small was here and considered this place important. But R1 and R4 are functional rather than evocative. R1 is a tool (portcullis mechanism). R4 is a view plus a data point (the tally stone). Stuart rooms exist to surprise you; R1 and R4 don't have surprises.

**Table (7/10):** Memory Fragments in every room is a correct design choice. The Memory Fragments for R2 (shrine arrangements), R3 (Velantha standing at the threshold), R5 (the chisel in the stone), and R6 (Velantha checking the gate latch before she left for the last time) are all specific and earned. R1's Memory Fragment (a woman's hands checking the gate latch) and R4's Memory Fragment (thirty years of scratching marks into stone) are good but they're both about Velantha rather than the congregation. The congregation has also had experiences in these rooms — thirty years' worth.

## P0 Issues

**P0.1 — Gully dwarves lack setting gloss on first use**

The background section introduces gully dwarves as the congregation without explaining why their thirty-year vigil is surprising. In Dragonlance, gully dwarves (Aghar) are considered incapable of organized thought, faith, or loyalty by every faction in the world — Knights, Wizards, Kender, even other dwarves. Their congregation is extraordinary precisely because no one believes they're capable of it. Without this gloss, the party might treat them as generic "little people" rather than as a community that has done something their world considers impossible.

**Fix:** Add one sentence to the background section: "Gully dwarves — Aghar, in the old tongue — are considered by Krynnish society to be incapable of sustained purpose, faith, or memory. The congregation in this gatehouse has maintained a daily practice for thirty years."

## Suggestions

1. Give Dova a presence in the adventure. She was the First-Keeper; she died here. There should be evidence of her in the crypt direction — a worn spot on the floor of R5 where she sat, a stone that's smoother than the others because she handled it daily for five years. Not a ghost, not a stat block. A worn spot.
2. The tally stone in R4 is the adventure's most quietly devastating object. The Memory Fragment says the habit "became prayer somewhere in the middle." That sentence should be in the read-aloud, not the Memory Fragment. It is the kind of thing that lands when the DM says it aloud, not when they read it to themselves while the players wait.
3. At least one of the unnamed congregation members should have a private reaction to the satchel that distinguishes them from the group. Not a name, not a stat block — a behavior. "One of them — the smallest — reaches for the satchel when Mig is distracted, pulls their hand back, looks at their hand as if surprised by it." That's an NPC.
