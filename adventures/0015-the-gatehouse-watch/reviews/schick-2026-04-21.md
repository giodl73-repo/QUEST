## Verdict: PASS

The satchel is the biography of a thirty-year vigil. The **V.S.** initials scratched into the leather — not stamped, not dyed, *scratched* — are exactly the kind of detail that carries everything. The Presence/Desire section ("the quality of readiness") is the best treasure temperament this workshop has written. Pass.

## Strongest axes

**Treasure (9/10):** The field satchel would kill this adventure if you removed it. Without the satchel, there is no Mig, no warmth anomaly, no V.S. mystery, no campaign relic chain, no reason the congregation has maintained this space for thirty years. The satchel is the dungeon's biography. The provenance chain (leatherworker → Velantha → Dova → eight more keepers → Mig) is fully specified; the Presence/Desire section is evocative without being mechanical ("it feels lighter than it should when picked up by someone in a hurry"); the reaction moment at R6 (herb smell through the threshold gap as the door opens for the first time) lands at exactly the right moment. Counter-evidence: the relic test has no mechanical cost — the satchel is entirely beneficial to own. Schick wants cost alongside benefit.

**Agency (8/10):** Route D — surrendering the building — is the only ending this campaign has written where the party's restraint is the treasure. Doing less is the optimal play. That is rare and correct. Counter-evidence: Route C (coexistence) is described as "messy and unstable" but the module doesn't give it a concrete physical image at the table. The DM is told it's possible but not shown what it looks like.

## Weakest axes

**Curse (6/10):** The relic test is morally interesting — use this for harm and it becomes ordinary leather — but it has no creeping cost. There is no cost to simply owning the satchel. The congregation's 30-year observation that "no keeper has died of fever" suggests the satchel protects its holder; this is all benefit. Schick wants the adventurer to feel the weight of the object even when it's doing them good. The field satchel has no weight yet. It will acquire weight as the campaign reveals what Velantha paid to make these objects meaningful — but in this adventure, that weight is absent.

**Dungeon (7/10):** The rooms express the artifact correctly. R2's warmth anomaly near Mig is the best treasure-architecture match — the room is cold, the artifact is warm, the warmth is localized to where the relic sits. R5's half-carved Mishakal symbol is the best room in the adventure (a thing stopped mid-completion whose incompleteness is the point). R6's herb smell through the threshold gap is the perfect reaction moment. Counter-evidence: R1, R3, and R4 don't express the satchel. R4's tally stone expresses time, not the relic. The upper third of the dungeon is architecturally disconnected from the treasure it houses.

## P0 Issues

**None.** The treasure design is sound at every required level. Manifest obligations are met. The reaction moment fires at the right location. The Presence/Desire section is specific enough for improvisation.

## Suggestions

1. The V.S. initials deserve a DC. Currently they appear in the room text without a check requirement — any player can see them. This is correct for the Adventure (first encounter with evidence should be accessible), but the letters should require a DC 11 Investigation from the outside; a DC 8 from someone who has turned the satchel over specifically to look at the underside. Two discovery paths, both accessible, both rewarding different kinds of attention.
2. The Presence/Desire section says the satchel is "warmest in the hands of people who are afraid and helping anyway." Dren Holt (the Fighter, who is afraid and helping) should get a specific note in Scene 2's GM Notes: "When Dren first takes the satchel — even just to examine it — it is noticeably warmer in his hands than in anyone else's. The DM does not explain this. The party will draw the conclusion eventually."
3. Route C needs one physical image. What does coexistence look like at the keystone? "Two services, shared space, neither community fully satisfied" is a consequence, not a scene. Schick wants to know: where do the gully dwarves sit during a human service? On the floor? In the back? Behind a partition? One sentence of image makes the coexistence route playable.
