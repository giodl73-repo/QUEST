## Verdict: PASS

The spec is clear. The pipeline path is obvious. Agency is the strongest axis in the campaign — three routes, real costs, genuine inversion. A DM picking this up can see exactly what they're building toward. My concerns are workload flags, not structural failures.

## Strongest axes

**Agency (9/10):** Route A/C/D are defined with explicit costs and gains for each. Route D's cost ("the town doesn't get the cathedral they planned") is specific. Route A's cost ("the truth was used as a weapon") is named. Route C's instability ("someone will always be unhappy") is honest. This is the clearest route-definition I've seen from this workshop. Counter-evidence: the routes are defined at finale level but mid-campaign agency (can the party skip sections? negotiate passage?) is not yet specified.

**Table (7/10):** The spec is usable. Section assignments are clear. The escalation shape (surface → middle → deep → choice) maps directly to session planning. Open questions are appropriately flagged rather than left implicit.

## Weakest axes

**Treasure (4/10):** No items means no loot tables means the DM has nothing to hand players when they ask "what do we find?" This is a workload problem — if treasure-forger is not given a campaign spine, each adventure will invent independently and the campaign won't cohere.

**Encounter (6/10):** "Not everyone she healed stayed grateful" (Bell Tower, 0017) is the spec's only encounter differentiation. The DM needs to know: what makes the Bell Tower group dangerous? Resentment? A specific grievance? A leader? The spec gives the adventure-slot its danger-signal but not its encounter-texture.

## P0 Issues

**P0.1 — NPC workload not flagged**
Seven adventures of congregation members as primary content means 15–25 named NPCs across the campaign, each needing an npc-architect file. The spec doesn't flag this. A DM running campaign-planner will not see this coming. **Fix:** Add a "Pipeline workload note" to the spec: "Each adventure requires 2–4 npc-architect files for congregation members. Prioritize arc-completion design for: the Transept Witness (0018), the Orders' man in the Sacristy (0019), and the Bell Tower leader (0017). These three NPCs carry the most narrative load."

## Suggestions

1. The spec says "the party has been sent for because no one else will come." This should be the first sentence a DM reads at the table — the hook that sets the party's relationship to the commission. Make sure it survives into the campaign spine as the patron's exact brief.
2. Route C (share) needs one more beat of specificity: what does "sharing" look like physically? Two services per day? Separate entrances? Without a concrete image, Route C will feel vague at the table compared to Routes A and D.
